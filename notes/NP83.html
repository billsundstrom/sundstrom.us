Nigel of CHESTER

[I007431]
In Norman Cheshire, the first Earl of Chester, Hugh d'Avranches split his estate into baronies. The Halton barony held precedent over all others and Nigel, the constable of Chester (Cheshire) became its first baron. Nigel erected a motte and bailey castle on Halton Hill around 1071 to keep watch over the Lancashire and Cheshire plains and the river estuary. The stone structured castle, as is seen remained today, would have started development in the later half of the 12th century; being gradually built upon, with each of the early barons undertaking various projects and adding to the work of his predecessor.

In 1115, Nigel's son, William Fitznigel, founded an Augustinian Priory at Runcorn. In 1134 the monks moved the priory to Norton, about three and a half miles away. The barons of Halton provided Norton Priory with substantial amounts of money until 1200. In 1391 the priory was raised to the higher status of abbey. In 1536 the monastery was dissolved, and a few years later the buildings and some of the monastic lands were sold to Sir Richard Brooke who converted the habitable part of the Abbey into a home for himself and his family.

Harry Kenneth CHIDLAW

[I072027]
Aurora - Ken Chidlaw , a former Potter & Mellen silversmith, continued making silver jewelry and art pieces at home after leaving the upscale jewelry and giftware firm to work for the Lincoln Electric Co. in the early 1950s.
Chidlaw , who retired from Lincoln Electric's research and development department in the mid-1990s, also tried his hand at woodworking and, more recently, glass blowing.
The 81-year-old Aurora resident died Wednesday at the Hospice of the Western Reserve in Cleveland. Memorial services will be at 4 p.m. today at the Church in Aurora, 146 S. Chillicothe Road, Aurora.
Chidlaw loved to travel and to try new things. He once made the impromptu announcement to his first wife: "We're going to Hawaii. A volcano's erupting." They made the trip, and Chidlaw took aerial photos of the bubbling lava to prove it.
"He didn't want to miss any thing," said his son, Scott of Elyria. "He took pictures of everything."
In his retirement, Chidlaw volunteered as a docent at the Holden Arboretum in Kirtland.
Harry Kenneth Chidlaw was born in Montana and grew up in Claremont, Calif., where his father taught him how to work with silver.
After graduating from Chaffee Junior College in 1942, Chidlaw joined the Navy for service in World War II. The Navy sent him to diesel mechanic school in Cleveland, where he met Joan Ford, the first of his three wives. He served on a submarine chaser in the Atlantic and on a support landing craft in the Pacific before returning to Cleveland.
Chidlaw , formerly of Lyndhurst, was a member of Lyndhurst Presbyterian Church.
His first wife died in 1985. Chidlaw and his second wife, the former Patricia Cathcart, were married for 10 years before her death in 1997.
In November 1998, he married the former Ann deHamel Horsey, whose daughter, the former Corinne deHamel, is married to his second wife's son, Robert Cathcart. The Cathcarts live in Chagrin Falls.
Besides his wife, son, stepson and stepdaughter, Chidlaw is survived by stepdaughters, Ellen S. Trezevant of Brussels, Belgium, Laura L. Dodge of Newport Beach, Calif., and JoAnne deHamel-Small of Chagrin Falls; five grandchildren; three step-grandchildren; two great-grandchildren; and two sisters. Chidlaw 's daughter, Martha Cooke, died two months ago.
Donations may be made to Holden Arboretum, 9500 Sperry Road, Kirtland 44094, or to Hospice of the Western Reserve, 300 East 185th St., Cleveland 44119.
The Plain Dealer, 10 September 10 2002

Edith Dunbar CHILDS

[I004027]
Mrs. Edith Dunbar (Childs) Miller, widow of Edwin Cyrus Miller of Haydenville, Mass., dropped dead from a heart attack in her home, Hillridge Lodge, at Haydenville, Friday morning, March 18th, 1932, aged about 63 years.
She was the daughter of the late Mr. and Mrs. Henry Childs of Northampton, Mass. Her husband died about eight years ago and was the well known owner of Hillside Orchards, one of the largest orchards in New England. He was long a vice-president and from 1911-13 president of the Miller Family Association of Northampton. The farm he owned was taken up in 1735 by his great-great grandfather John Miller the youngest son of Ebenezer and Sarah (Allen) Miller of Northampton and grandson of Patience Northampton in 1654 and Northfleld, Mass. in 1672.
Stephen Miller, who came in 1814 from Williamsburg. Mass. to Middlebury, now in Wyoming County. N. Y., was the eldest son of John Miller above, and his wife Martha Root, both born in Northampton.
At a great reunion of the Miller family (250 members present) held in Village Hall, Wyoming, N. Y., in August, 1914, celebrating the 100th anniversary of the coming of Stephen Miller and family to Middlebury. Mr. and Mrs. E. Cyrus Miller were present and remarks were made by him.
They were members of the Haydenville Congregational church and active in church and social affairs.
Mrs. Miller leaves two daughters, Charlotte, wife of Alfred Dimes of Hartford, Conn., and Miss Gladys Miller, a professor of the Boston Conservatory of Music, a grandson, Edwin Dimes of Hartford, and a niece. Mrs. Edward Phelphs of Northampton.
Funeral services were held from the Haydenville Congregational church, Sunday afternoon, March 20th, Rev. Ribert H. Life, pastor, officiating assisted by Rev. James Henry Larson of Northampton. Burial was made in High Street cemetery, Haydenville.
The Wyoming Reporter, Wyoming, NY, 20 April 1932

Robert Dressel CHILDS

[I020123]
Robert Dressel Childs passed away at home on August 19, 2014 after a courageous battle with leukemia. Bob was born in Tulare, California on March 19, 1927 to Evelyn Dressel Childs and Edwin Robert Childs . The family moved to Santa Ana where Bob completed his kindergarten through junior college education. An avid sportsman throughout his entire life, Bob most loved football during his earlier years and played for Santa Ana High School, Santa Ana Junior College, and Stanford. Bob served in the Naval Air Corps from January 1945 through the summer of 1946, when he returned to Santa Ana Junior College and was elected freshman class president. Bob enrolled at Stanford in the fall of 1947, where he joined the Phi Kappa Psi fraternity and played defensive end and tackle for the Stanford football team. It was at Stanford that Bob met Beverly Bond. They were married their senior year and graduated together in 1950. Bob earned his BA in Biology, followed by an MBA. The couple returned to Southern California where Bob worked in the insurance industry for more than 40 years. He was an active Stanford alumnus and chaired the Orange County Stanford Conference. Bob' s knowledge and passion for history and the humanities took him on many adventurous travels worldwide, always with his wife, Bev, with their children, and finally with their grandchildren. His children, grandchildren, and all of their friends will remember Bob for his generosity, his enthusiasm, his love of the outdoors and his commitment to a physically active lifestyle. Bob is survived by his wife of 65 years, Beverly; his sister, Marian Greer; his children, Gail Omohundro (Tom), Blake Childs , Mike Childs (Cindy); his grandchildren, Mark Omohundro, Amy Omohundro McNulty, Gavin Childs , and Aaron Child; and his step-grandchildren, Christine, Andika and John Omohundro. He will be remembered and missed by many. The family is planning a private burial at sea.
