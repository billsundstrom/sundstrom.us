John E. GRENZEBACH

[I034173]
John Grenzebach , 72, a professional fund-raiser here for more than 35 years, died last Thursday at St. Thomas in the U.S. Virgin Islands.
He was a longtime Evanston resident but most recently had lived on Anna Maria Island in Florida.
He was chairman of John Grenzebach & Associates, Inc., a firm he founded in 1960 that specialized in institutional fund-raising for colleges and universities. From 1983 to 1985, Mr. Grenzebach also was chairman of the American Association of Fund-Raising Counsel, Inc., based in New York. He was considered a pioneer in the fund-raising industry, and he wrote articles and lectured on the subject.
Before 1960 he was executive vice president of Beaver Associates Inc., a professional fund-raising management firm in Chicago. He had served on the boards of a number of educational and community organizations. He had been chairman of the executive committee of Evanston Township High School, president of the Unitarian Church of Evanston, and a board member of Kendall College, North Shore Community Music Center and Allendale School for Boys.
Mr. Grenzebach was born in Buffalo, N.Y., and educated at the University of Buffalo, the University of North Carolina and the University of Munich in West Germany.
Surviving are his wife, Marilynn; three sons, John C., Eric and Martin, and three grandchildren.
Memorial services will be held in Chicago at a later date.
Chicago Sun-Times, 25 February 1987

GRAY , ALICE RHEES GRIFFIN June 24, 1913-January 10, 2005. Alice Rhees Griffin Gray passed away at Willamette View Manor's Health Care Center in the wee small hours of Jan. 10, 2005. She was born June 24, 1913, in Portland, to Mary Chambers Griffin and Frank Loxley Griffin, Sr. Her father, affectionately known as "Griff," was a mathematics professor at Reed College and later served as the college's president. Alice was the youngest of four. She graduated from Washington High School in 1931. Classmates selected her clean, classic pen and ink entry as the winning artwork to be printed and used by the 1931 graduating class. She received a Bachelor of Science degree (home economics) from OSU in 1935, and married Charles Marshall Gray on June 24, 1936, in a garden ceremony in the side yard of her parent's Eastmoreland home. The two moved to California during World War II and she volunteered as a "Grey Lady," assisting in the military hospitals. Alice and her husband expanded their family with a daughter in 1949 and a son in 1956. An avid "birder" and a member of the Audubon Society, Alice enjoyed feeding and banding birds who came into her yard to eat and bathe in the pond. Several baby birds were raised to maturity including a Lawrence's Goldfinch, sparrows and cowbirds. Later years were spent in a home in Dunthorpe, near Lake Oswego, where she continued her interest in local birds. Alice brought beauty to her one acre garden, adding little paths and woodsy garden benches among the ferns and flowers. She gave service to others continually, in many ways, both collaboratively and privately. After moving to Willamette View Manor, 18 years ago, she served on many committees and rendered service to others daily. Her room at Willamette View Manor looked out on an ever changing expanse of the Willamette River. She enjoyed watching the boats, sea planes and wildlife of the river and Elk Rock. She is survived by her daughter, Cheri Lynn Haner, of Wood Village; her son, David Edwin Gray , of Hillsboro, and grandchildren, Rebecca Haner, Jeffrey Gray , Russell Gray , and Teresa Jeanne (TJ) Gray . Instructions were left for no funeral, but a celebration of life party will be held at Willamette View Manor in The Commons area, Saturday, Jan. 22, 2005, from 11 a.m. to 2 p.m.

Frank Loxley GRIFFIN

[I098818]
Frank L. Griffin, a nationally recognized actuary who had designed private pension plans for a number of top U.S. corporations, died Monday in his Southwest Portland home of complications from Alzheimer's disease. He was 81.
A private family funeral will be at 11 a.m. Thursday in the Adams Chapel of River View Cemetery. Entombment will be in River View Cemetery Mausoleum.
Mr. Griffin was born Oct. 27, 1909, in Williamstown, Mass. His father, Frank Loxley Griffin, was the first professor appointed to the Reed College faculty in 1911 and later served as president of the college.
After graduating from Reed in 1931, Mr. Griffin began working for Prudential Insurance Co. in Newark, N.J.
After serving in the U.S. Navy during World War II, he moved to the Chicago area, where he became head of the pension consulting department for the firm of Marsh and McLennan, supervising the Midwest area.
In 1950, Mr. Griffin joined the staff of Wyatt Co. He led the company's Chicago operations for a number of years and was chairman of the board at the time of his retirement in 1971.
Before retiring, Mr. Griffin became owner, publisher and editor of a weekly newspaper in Douglas County, Nev. He wrote several books on a variety of his favorite subjects, including black and white photography, poetry, philosophy and conservation.
During the mid-1970s, he served on a special advisory group to Congress that examined financial issues related to the Social Security program. He was president of the Conference of Actuaries in 1969-70.
Mr. Griffin had served on the boards of the Society of Actuaries, the Conference of Actuaries in Public Practice, the American Academy of Actuaries and the Wyatt Co. He had served as a trustee for the Reed Institute and was awarded the Foster-Schulz Reed College Alumni Association Distinguished Service Award in 1987.
He had lived in the Portland area since 1978.
Survivors include his wife, Myrthle; daughters, Barbara McDougal Dan of Las Vegas, Nev., and Margaret Ann Perkins of Antioch, Calif.; sons, Alan C. of Santa Cruz, Calif., Peter A. of Sacramento, Calif., John L. of Lake Oswego and Christopher B. of San Antonio, Texas; sisters, Helen Griffin Tibbits of Boston and Ruth Stephensen and Alice Gray, both of Milwaukie; seven grandchildren; and two great-grandchildren.
The Oregonian, 1 Nov 1990, pg C13

Frank Loxley GRIFFIN

[I098806]
Dr. Frank Loxley Griffin, 88, former president and professor of mathematics at Reed College who was considered by many as the most influential teacher of mathematics in the United States, died Sunday, Nov. 9, at a local hospital.

He had been hospitalized since Sept 19. His death ended a career that spanned the entire history of Reed College, and produced a major influence on the teaching of mathematics.

Dr. Griffin was the first faculty member selected to teach at Reed. William Trufant Foster, first president Reed, hired him away from Williams College, Williamstown, Mass. Dr. Griffin welcomed the freedom of Reed offered to experiment with new ideas in teaching.

He taught at Reed for 41 years, from 1911 to 1952. He emerged from retirement in 194 to become college president and steer Reed through a period of perplexing controversy growing from the appearance of a number of Reed faculty members and students before the House of Representatives' Un-American Activities Committee.

Following his firs and second retirements, he was a visiting professor at several American colleges and universities.

In 1921 his firs book, "Introduction to Mathematical Analysis: was acclaimed by "Scientific American" magazine, which said, "if we had our way, every teacher of mathematics would be obliged to read this book, and every person with responsibility for laying out of mathematical instruction in any of our colleges would be obliged to read a chapter from it every morning before breakfast." This textbook became the first American one adopted by a European university. He wrote six others, and many articles on mathematics and education.

He was an advocate of teaching excellence and was a self-announced enemy of sloth and "Dr. Dull and Dreary." Dr. Griffin specialized in helping students, who had little aptitude for or interest in learning mathematics. He was acclaimed for making it so interesting that they ended up begging for homework.

In 1938 he was honored by the General Education Board of the Rockefeller Foundation, which sent four young mathematicians to work under him and learn his teaching principles.
Born in Topeka, Kan., in 1881, he attended the University of Chicago, and received bachelor's, master's and doctor's degrees magna cum laude. He was awarded special honors for work in mathematics and astronomy. He received special citations for public service and education from the University of Oregon, the Oregon Academy of Science and the University of Chicago Alumni Association.
Dr. Griffin was first vice president and member of the board of governors of the Mathematical Association of America, and the Association of University Professors.
He was president of the Oregon Academy of Science, a governor of the City Club of Portland, a regent of Multnomah College for 25 years, a fellow of the American Association for the Advancement of Science, a trustee of the Oregon Society of the Sons of the American Revolution, and a member of the Highland Baptist Church.
His wife, Mary Chambers Griffin, died Sept. 30, 1964. Dr. Griffin is survived by children: Mrs. Clark Tibbitts, Rockville, Md., Mrs. Howard Stephenson, Portland, Mrs. C. Marshall Gray, Lake Oswego, and Frank L. Griffin Jr., Western Springs, Ill., 12 grandchildren and 15 great grandchildren.
Interment will be private. A memorial service will be held at Reed College in late November.
The Oregonian, 10 Nov 1969, p15

Helen J. GRIFFIN

[I098819]
Helen Griffin Tibbitts, 86, former executive secretary of the behavioral science and biostatistics fellowship review committees at the National Institutes of Health, died of a heart attack Dec. 19 at a nursing home in Newton, Mass.
Dr. Tibbitts was born in Portland, Ore., and she graduated from Reed College. She received a master's degree and a doctorate in sociology from the University of Chicago.
She came to Washington in 1934 as an employment studies analyst with the Emergency Relief Administration. From 1935 to 1937, she worked on national health surveys for the U.S. Public Health Service in Washington and Detroit. She was a homemaker in Michigan from 1937 to 1947, then from 1947 to 1949 was director of public health statistics in Oregon.
In 1949, Dr. Tibbitts returned to the Washington area. She worked for the Office of Defense Mobilization, the Public Health Service and NIH, where she was executive secretary of the nursing research study section in the division of research grants. In 1966, she was appointed executive secretary of the behavioral science and biostatistics fellowship review committees. She retired in 1971.
In retirement, Dr. Tibbitts lectured on sociology at American University.
She was a member of Cedar Lane Unitarian Church in Bethesda.
A former resident of Rockville, she moved to Newton in 1987.
Her husband, Clark Tibbitts, died in 1985.
Survivors include three daughters, Alice Mailhot of Lansing, Mich., Helen Jean Thiebaux of Fort Washington and Ann Tibbitts Schulz of Barrington, N.H.; two sisters, Alice Gray and Ruth Stephenson, both of Portland; 12 grandchildren; and 12 great-grandchildren.

Joseph Alden GRIFFIN

Joseph Alden Griffin, son of Miles Flint and Cynthia Louisa (Alden) Griffin, was born in Somerville, Mass., February 3, 1853. His father was the son of Jonathan and Persis (Flint) Griffin, and a descendant of Matthew Griffin, an early settler in Charlestown, Mass., and a soldier in King Philipʼs War. Through his mother, who was a daughter of Col. Joseph Alden and Lucy (Warner) Alden, he traced his ancestry to John Alden of the "Mayflower" company.
His early education was received at Woburn, Mass., and he attended Washburn College, Topeka, Kans., for a year before entering Yale as a Sophomore in 1872. He won a second prize in English composition that year, and received an oration appointment and a first Winthrop Prize in Junior year. His Senior appointment was a dispute.
After taking his degree from Yale he taught for a year in General Russellʼs Collegiate and Commercial Institute in New Haven, spent a similar period as principal of the high school at Warren, Mass., and then became instructor in Latin and mathematics at Allen Academy, Chicago, Ill. He continued in this position until June, 1880, in the meantime reading law and attending lectures at the Union College of Law. He was admitted to the Illinois Bar June 16, 1880, and practiced in Chicago until 1908. He spent several years in the office of William Vocke, and was associated with the firm of Campbell & Custer from 1887 to 1906, then becoming a member of the firm of Custer, Goddard & Griffin. He had made a specialty of equity law. His home was at Evanston, Ill., from 1890 to 1908, when he removed to Madison, Wis., and opened an office for the general practice of law. His death occurred June 16, 1921, at his home, Quarry Hill Farm, near Madison. He attended the Congregational Church, and was a member of the Chicago Bar Association.
Mr. Griffin was married July 26, 1883, in Warren, Mass., to Angie Cornelia, daughter of William L. and Sarah (Nutting) Powers. She survives him with their only child, Clara Adelaide, and he also leaves a brother, James F. Griffin, of Los Angeles, Calif. James A. Valentine, ʼ02, is a cousin.
