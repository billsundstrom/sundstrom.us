He was a United Methodist Minister who worked from 1946-1956 at Whitneyville Larger Parish in Pennsylvania, from 1956-1962 at Baker Memorial United Methodist Church in East Aurora, from 1962-81 in Hamburg United Methodist Church and from 1981-1995 at First United Methodist Church in Perry.

He was an active member of Christian Educators Fellowship of Western New York and National, and was former director for the camping ministry at Camp Asbury, Waston Homestead in Painted Post and Cherry Creek Camp.
He volunteered with his family for more than 15 years at the work camp mission in West Virginia, was involved in Heifer International Missions in Massachussetts, the International Christian Youth Exchange Program for more than 15 years and was former superintendent of Silver Lake Institute.
Mr. Finch also was a former member of Perry Rotary Club and Grange in Pennsylvania, where he held top degrees and leadership.
His wife, Barbara Finch , who he married July 8, 1948, died Jan. 21, 2005. He was father of the late Sara Finch and brother of the late Davis, Robert and Gertrude Finch .

He was employed by Arizona Edison, Arizona Public Service and Southwest Gas companies until his retirement on Dec. 31, 1987. He was one of the last drivers for the Bisbee-Warren Busline before transferring to Douglas as customer serviceman.

He loved Bisbee, Cochise County and the people he met in his years working for the utilities companies. He looked forward to the Cochise County Fair every year. For several years, he was superintendent, along with his wife, Clara, for the fair mineral exhibits.
He is survived by his wife of 52 years, Clarabell Wright Finn; sons, Ray Finn, Fred (Kim) Finn, Richard Finn, and James (Cheryl) Finn; brother James Franklin (Becky) Finn; eight grandchildren; and two great-grandchildren. [S60] The Finney Family of Lenton.


John Finney emigrated to New England between July 1637 and December 1638. He was assigned land at Webb's Field in Plymouth on 12 December 1639, the grant was confirmed on 6 June 1640. On 16 September 1641 John and his brother Robert were each granted six acres of upland abutting the brook that comes from Fresh Lake, and John received another grant of fifty acres at Pausatuke Neck on 5 June 1666. The court confirmed the grant at Gassuntaguanumcke Neck on South Sea. 7 July 1668.

John was admitted a freeman of the colony on 20 August 1644. He held many positions of trust, beginning with appointment as constable at Plymouth on 7 March 1642. The following year he was include on a list of those able to bear arms from the town of Plymouth. he was collector of the excise at Plymouth 1646-1648 and was three times appointed surveyor of the highways in 1646,1654 and 1668. On 24 February 1652 he was chosen to lay out the highway from Plymouth to Sandwich with twelve other men.

John Finney removed to Barnstable before 29 August 1652 when he was admitted to Rev John Laathrops church. In Barnstable he was appointed exciseman in 1661, inspector of ordinaries in 1671 and constable in 1675. John was frequently a juryman in Plymouth And Barnstable.

On 1 March 1658/9 Isaac Robinson and Gyles Rickard complained to the court in behalf of two children of Henry Coggins, deceased, who were living in the home of John Finney at Barnstable, being the children of his second wife Abigail. The complaint was recorded three days after the death of 'Thomas Coggins, one of Henry Coggins Sons. The exact nature of the complaint is not stated, but it was claimed that the Coggins children had suffered wrong in sundry respects. The case was referred to the May session of the court. In the mean while, John was ordered to keep his stepson. apparently John Coggins, in school all the time excepting six days, and to prepare an account of the childrenʼs estate. John Coggins was eventually placed under the guardianship of Captain James Cudworth and Isaac Robinson, and on 7 June 1659 John Finney surrendered all of Henry Coggins lands at Barnstable to the guardians.

Late in his life, John Finney became interested in the Mount Hope lands, now Bristol RI. John and his sons Jonathan and Jeremiah were original grantees of Bristol on 1 September 1681. The last mention of him is in a deed dated 7 February 1682 in which he disposed of his house and thirty acres of lands in Bristol on the Swansea River to his son Jonathan.

Joseph FISER

[I078689]
Joseph Fiser, son of Vencil and Frances Fiser, was born March 16, 1868, in Manitowoc, Wisc. When a child of two his father died, leaving him, a sister, Anna and a brother, Vencil.
When he was about four years old the children with their mother and stepfather, Anton Lax, moved to a homestead near Cuba. Twelve years later the family, including two half-sisters, Julia and Emma came to Washington County to a farm 6 miles east of Mahaska.
November 18, 1891, he was united in holy wedlock to Anna Mabel Tuma. Mr. and Mrs. Fiser began their wedded life on a farm adjoining that of his parents. On this farm they continued to reside for over 53 years. During this period eight children, three sons and five daughters, were born and reared.
In 1944 they purchased a home in Mahaska. Here Mr. Fiser passed away June 16, 1952, at the age of 84 years and 3 months. He was preceded in death by his parents, one sister, Anna (Moe C. Melker); one brother, Vencil; one daughter, Sylvia (Mrs. Herbert Rose) and one granddaughter, Norma Hubka.
Those left to mourn his passing are his wife, Anna; sons, Louis and Elmer of Mahaska, and Lud of Manhattan; four daughters, Lillie (Mrs. E. E. Wells), Narka; Hilda, (Mrs. Rudy Hubka), Denver, Colo.; Olive (Mrs. A. H. Taylor), Mahaska; Mabel (Mrs. Robert Meyers), York, Neb.; two step-sisters, Mrs. Emma Miller of Sacramento, Cal., and Mrs. Julia Tuma, Fairbury, Neb.; twenty-two grandchildren, twelve great-grandchildren and many other relatives and friends.
Joseph Fiser was a true American pioneer, devoted to his family and home. He always enjoyed company. The friendly attitude and respect his neighbors had for him were indicative of the interest he had in them and the respect he held for them. During his boyhood days while residing near Cuba, it was not uncommon for him and his stepfather to travel many miles by ox team. Typical was a four-day trip to Waterville, the nearest point where grain could be milled.
He was one of the pioneer farmers of Washington county. He owned and operated the first self-tying binder in this county. He was very fond of horses; he enjoyed and took great pride in working with and caring for them. Mr. Fiser will always be remembered as a devoted husband and father, true neighbor, and as an individual who has contributed much to the welfare of this fine community.
He was a member of the ZCBJ lodge. Services were held Friday afternoon at the Presbyterian church, the Rev. Harry H. Hirsch conducting the services. Burial was made in Mahaska cemetery.
Belleville Telescope, 26 June 1952
