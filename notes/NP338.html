John LYMAN

[I012644]
Lieut. John Lyman was the son of Richard Lyman, one of the original
proprietors of Hartford, and Sarah Osborne. He came to New E ngland in "Lion" with his father in November 1631, and in 1636 made the overlan d march with Rev. Thomas Hooker's company to Hartford. A boy of thirteen at thi s time, "he doubtless was given frequent
opportunities to distinguish himself in rounding up the cattle, and the strategy thus learned stood him in good stea d, perhaps, when as a
lieutenant he commanded the Northampton men in the famo us fight" at Turner's Falls on the Connecticut River above Deerfield on May 18, 1676, during the Indian troubles known as King Philip's War.

Edward Joseph LYNCH

[I023122]
The fifth and youngest child of an Irish immigrant, John Lynch and his American-born wife, Elizabeth Owens, Edward Lynch grew up on farms in the Hudson Valley of New York State and in Long Island, New York during the Depression. Despite his poverty, his father sent him and two of his siblings through college. Edward graduated from Pratt Institute with a degree in Chemical Engineering at the age of 20 and was immediately inducted into the U.S. Army. He served as a chemist at what is now the Los Alamos National Laboratory for the duration of the war working on the purification of plutonium. At the end of the war he attended the University of California at Berkeley, where he earned a Ph.D. in Chemical Engineering. He joined the faculty at Stanford University after graduation, and in 1964 left to work in the private sector. He married and had three children. In 1973, at the age of 48, Edward Lynch was diagnosed with advanced metastatic colon cancer and he died at 51.
