Flo Nancy STEPHENS

[I062194]
Daughter of Alonzo F. Stephens and Cora Charlotte "Lottie" Taylor.
Flo lived in the Jesup area since she was 5 years old. She was born in South Dakota on a farm, November 6, 1885, to Alonzo and Lottie Stevens. She attended Jesup Community School for two years when a child, then moved back into the country, where she atended country school, through grade 8.
On November 12, 1902, she married Hughey Fuller.
She has survived two of her children, Harold and Walter, but has a daughter, Gertrude Ammons, living in Waterloo.
She "can't count" all of her great-grandchildren and great, great-grandchildren.
Hughey was killed in a truck-train accident in 1966.
She recalls the first car she rode in was a Stanley Steamer. However, she gave up driving last month on her 95th birthday.
Flo remembers driving a school bus for Jesup school . The school furnished the bus, an open wagon with hard wooden benches for seats, but the driver furnished the team of horses to pull it. During the winter, she heated rocks in her oven, then covered them with rags, and placed them in the wagon bottom to help keep the children warm beneath their blankets and heavy clothes.

He studied foreign languages all his lifetime, mastering French, German, and Spanish. He continued his study of Spanish at Foothill College, DeAnza College, and San Jose State University. Among his many talents were his love an appreciation of music and his mastery of games. He was born in Detroit, Michigan, and graduated from Cass Tech High School. He attended the University of Michigan and transferred to Stanford University, where he received his undergraduate degree in Psychology and then his Doctorate of Medicine in 1969. During this time, he married his college sweetheart, Stephanie Alexander, who graduated form Stanford University with a degree in Nursing. His internship was spent at Georgetown University Hospital in pediatrics. He returned again to Stanford to complete his residency in Child Psychiatry. He served in the U.S. Army as a Major for three years at the 2nd General Hospital in Landstuhl, Germany. In 1977, he stared his private practice in Child and Adolescent Psychiatry in Palo Alto. He cared for patients at the CAPI Unit at San Jose Hospital for nearly twenty years. During this time he also was a consultant to the Star House Residential Group Homes. Currently he was associated with the Pacific Biobehavioral Group in San Jose with Dr. Saul Wasserman and Dr. William Cherry. He is survived by his wife Stephanie, son Gregory Stephens of Sacramento, daughter and son-in-law Cynthia and Rich Wilde of Redwood City, and four grandchildren; a brother, Gary Stephens, sister, Barbara Bell, two nephews and one niece, all of Michigan. Margery STEPHENS

[I040085]
Christopher Avery, the emigrant ancestor and progenitor of the Avery family, was born in England about 1590. He was a weaver by trade, and came to this country and located at Gloucester, Massachusetts, where he was selectman in 1646, 1652 and 1654. At a court in Salem he took the freeman's oath, June 29, 1692, and was chosen clerk of the band, constable, and clerk of the market. His wife did not come to this country. In 1658 he sold lands at Gloucester and removed to Boston, where on the 16th of March, 1658-9 he purchased land, a small lot, about twenty-six by forty-six feet. It was located in what is now the centre of the post-office building, facing on Devonshire street. The famous old spring, which gave the name to Spring Lane and which is now preserved under the post-office, was near. This Avery plot was a part of, or at least adjoined, the site of two notable resorts of later days--the well known restaurant whence first came the famous "Julien soup", and the "Stackpole House," not much less famous. The Winthrop estate was not far away, and near by , in after years, Benjamin Franklin was born. Christoper Avery did not long retain this property, for March 22, 1663, he sold land to Ambrose Dew, for forty pounds. There had evidently been no increase of value in the five years that he had held possession. After being owned by two or three different persons, it was bought by Mr. Stackpole about 1790. Christopher Avery now followed his son James to Connecticut, and August 8 1665, purchased a house, orchard and lot of Robert Burrows in New London. Here he claimed exemption from watching and training, on account of age, in June 1667, and was made freeman of the colony October, 1669. He died March 12, 1670, by Minor diary.
