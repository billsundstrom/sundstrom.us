Raymond James NEWCOMB

[I078600]
Raymond J. Newcomb 82, of What Cheer died Wednesday, December 7, 2011 at his home. A graveside funeral service will be held at 10:00 A.M. at the Barnes City Cemetery in Barnes City on Monday, December 12, 2011. No visitation is planned. Memorial contributions may be made to the family.
Raymond James Newcomb was born June 7, 1929 in What Cheer, Iowa the son of John and Mary Shirlaw Newcomb. Ray attended grade school at North School, Brick School and High School all in What Cheer. He graduated from What Cheer High School in 1947. He also attended Linotype School at Charles City in 1946. Ray was married August 22, 1950 to Ramona Vermillion. She died March 28, 1987. They spent all of their lives in What Cheer and vicinity except for brief periods when they lived in Hobbs, Deming, and Silver City, New Mexico and Denver City, Texas. For a number of years he worked as a linotype operator and printer at various Iowa weekly newspapers. Including the New Sharon Star where he was part owner, at Keokuk County News; Sigourney News Review; Dows; Tama; Deep River; and Oskaloosa Tribune Press. Ray worked at the Patriot-Chronicle from 1942 until 1950. Raymond and Ramona became publishers of the Patriot-Chronicle in What Cheer in 1971 where he continued as an owner and operator of the paper for 25 years. He was a member of the What Cheer United Methodist Church.
Survivors include a son, John and Melody Newcomb of Florida, a daughter and long-time friend, Charlotte Kitzman and John Null of What Cheer; seven grandchildren, Shelley Kitzman, John Newcomb Jr., Travis Newcomb, Rachel Tipton, Michael Decker, Brandi McGinnis and Chancey Parker; twenty one great-grandchildren; and one great-great-grandson; two sisters-in-law, Anita Vermillion and Delores Newcomb, both of What Cheer; one brother-in-law, Everett James of Sigourney and several nieces, nephews and cousins. One of his great-granddaughters, Ava Ray Schultz, was born at his home December 10, 2007 in What Cheer. Three of his great-granddaughters spent many years living with Ray or seeing him every day, Haley Kitzman, Sydney Schultz and Ava Ray Schultz. Maxine Dugger of What Cheer, a surviving cousin was like a sister to Raymond, as they grew up together and were very close all of his life.
Preceding him in death are his parents; wife Ramona; brothers: Jack Newcomb and David Newcomb and an infant brother; sisters: Juanita Chapman, Elaine Colbert, Leola James, Shirley McKeever and an infant sister; three grandchildren and one great-grandchild.

Harry V. NEWELL

[I050336]
Harry V. Newell, 56 years old, Captain of the Ketchikan Fire Department was killed on April 7, 1955 fighting a fire in the basement repair shop of the Smith Electric Company in Ketchikan.
Three other firefighters and police officers were overcome by smoke and gases, but were revived shortly after. Newell who was wearing a Scott Air Pak had been in the fire building for an estimated 20 minutes before he was rescued and brought outside. Records regarding the fire do not indicate the cause nor the damage.
Harry Newell had been a Captain in the Ketchikan Fire Department for a number of years, was a past governor of the Moose Lodge, a past ruler of the Elks Lodge, and a member of the Eagles Lodge.
At the time of his death he was survived by a separated wife, Mary, two sons of Ketchikan, Bob and Harry F., and a daughter then of Vancouver, Washington, Mrs. John Connors. Harry had lived in Ketchikan for 32 years.

Alex NEWPORT-BERRA

[I109819]
Former Corvallis resident Alex Newport-Berra, 33, of Ashland left us on July 20, 2014, in the midst of the beauty and majesty of the Rocky Mountains outside Silverton, Colorado. He lived every day, every hour, indeed, every minute, to its fullest taking in the joy, the beauty, the wonder and the magic of everyone and everything around him.
Alex has always been an explorer. His adventures began in the Willamette Valley amongst the splendor of McDonald Forest, where he was raised. The wilderness of his homeland inspired Alex to seek natureʼs beauty everywhere, and his travels took him to places like Hawaii, Utah, Montana, Italy and Costa Rica. Each journey was accompanied and inspired by sport, and no matter which sport Alex embraced - climbing, running, cycling, yoga, surfing, skiing or hiking - he did it with focus, passion and a respect for, and love of, nature. He found both solace and wild inspiration in the mountains, hills, rocks, dirt and waves. Alex was a driven perfectionist who acutely trained his body and mind, yet also understood the importance of being still, and practiced quietude with grace.
Alex was, and continues to be, an incredibly gifted and compassionate teacher. He most recently taught math at the Siskiyou School, mentored and tutored youth in the Ashland area, and taught yoga at Bikram Yoga of Ashland. While living in Corvallis, he worked as a teacherʼs aide at Linus Pauling Middle School and at Bikram Yoga of Corvallis. In these roles, and in his everyday interactions, he inspired and empowered youth and adults alike - to believe in themselves, to challenge themselves, and to appreciate and strive to better understand the physical and spiritual world around them. Alex listened to and learned from those he touched, and drew great inspiration from his students, friends and family. This mutual inspiration is best captured by one of his very own quotations: "May each pilot their own ship, and may your lifeʼs passion be a wind to fill othersʼ sails."
Alex was a writer and storyteller; his words are sincere, insightful, creative, always entertaining and often funny. Through his stories, poetry and other writings, he shares his appreciation for nature, and his wisdom of the rhythm of the physical and spiritual world. Anyone who feels his presence can sense infinite wisdom, yet an uncanny, youthful naiveté. Much of his writing can be found on his blog: https://alexnberra.wordpress.com.
Having graduated from Crescent Valley High School in 1999, Alex attended Oregon State University, and later graduated from the University of Hawaii.
Alex was a committed and loving son, brother and uncle. He is survived by his parents, Pat Newport and Harold Burton (Buzz) Berra Jr.; brother, Adam Newport-Berra; sister, McHale Newport-Berra; brother-in-law, Samidh Chakrabarti; and niece, Kaya Chakra-Berra.
Alex's physical body left us while doing what he loved. Words cannot express how much he will be missed, but Alex continues to live on through his infinite manifestations in nature. Alex invites you to look to the trees, mountains and waves, to find the magic in your own life.
A gathering to honor Alex will take place at 11 a.m. Saturday, Aug. 9, at his home near McDonald Forest in Corvallis. Since there is no parking at the house, please meet at 10:30 a.m. in the Crescent Valley High School parking lot - there will be plenty of shuttles to take you the mile to the Newport-Berrasʼ home on Jackson Creek Drive.
Corvallis Gazette-Times, 2 August 2014

Frank Arthur NEWTON

[I065549]
Supervisor for Commonwealth and Southern Corp. Dies-----Headed Research Unit
Frank A. Newton of Summit, N.J., rate supervisor for the Commonwealth and Southern Corporation, one of the nation's largest utility holding company systems, died yesterday of a heart attack in Peoria, Ill., according to word received here. He was 61 years old.
Born in Sparta, Wis., Mr. Newton attended the College of Engineering at the University of Wisconsin. After graduation he joined the United States Reclamation Service in Colorado, later going to the Geological Service and the Wisconsin Railroad Commission. In 1912 he became a staff member of Hodenpyl Hardy & Co., a utility consulting firm.
Upon formation of Commonwealth & southern in 1929, Mr. Newton was placed in general supervisory charge of rates for the company. He was chairman of the rate research committee of the Edison Electric Institute for more than ten years and was a frequent contributor to technical and trade publications.
He leaves a widow, Bertha Richardson Newton; two daughters, Mrs. Gladys Newton Handley and Miss Marjorie Jean Newton; a son, Robert H. Newton of Jackson, Mic., and two granddaughters.
The New York Times Saturday, 17 November 1945
