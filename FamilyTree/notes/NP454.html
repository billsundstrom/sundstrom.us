Katherine R. "Kay" Harkness, 96, of Severna Park, Maryland died in her home on January 14, 2016, following a long illness. She had family with her to the end. She was born to Fred and Viola Rimmer in Iredell County, North Carolina on March 28, 1919. She and her five younger siblings lived and grew up in Statesville, North Carolina. She earned a bachelor始s degree in sociology from the Women始s College of North Carolina (now UNC Greensboro), and then went to Hartford Seminary in Connecticut where she earned a master始s degree in Christian Education. There she met the Rev. John D. Harkness and the two were married on July 26, 1941. They embarked on a fifteen year adventure in India and Pakistan as missionaries of the Presbyterian Church. This adventure began with their ship docking across the bay from Pearl Harbor during the attack on December 7, 1941. They ministered to and served local Christian congregations in various locations in the subcontinent up to and following the Partition of India and Pakistan. During these years Kay gave birth to her four children. After their service overseas, the family returned to this country in 1956 and moved to Akron, Ohio where John served as pastor of the Margaret Park Presbyterian Church. Kay was an elementary school teacher in the Akron Public Schools. In 1969, the family moved to Severna Park, where her husband served as Associate Pastor at Woods Memorial Presbyterian Church until his retirement. Kay taught in the Anne Arundel Public School System from 1969 until her retirement in 1985. Kay embraced life fully, possessed of a uniquely positive outlook graced by her unswerving faith which she conveyed to all who knew her. She loved her family, doted on her grandchildren, and took pride in everyone始s achievements. She was an excellent cook and loved to bring everybody around her table. Her home was a place of hospitality, books, beautiful music, bible study, prayer, wisdom, and inspiration. She was an active member of First Presbyterian in Annapolis and Woods Memorial and served in both as an elder and teacher. Kay was also active in the leadership of Presbyterian Women and Church Women United. Her energies extended to promoting Habitat for Humanity and preparing meals for the Lighthouse Shelter. She was preceded in death by her husband, who passed away on May 13, 1980. She is survived by her four children, John (Suzanne), Esther (Richard Keck), David (Martha) and Deborah (David Hasek), her brother Harold, and sisters Hazel and Eloise: by six grandchildren: Peter (Brenda), Sarah (Ethon), William (Monet), Mary, John, and Andrew and two great-grandchildren: Dylan and Weston. She is survived by her step grandchild Wendy and step great-grandchildren Regan and Jenna. She leaves behind her devoted caregiver of the last seven years, Jacqueline Afrifa, and countless friends. Edward Dale RINTYE

[I086408]
Ed Rintye: Teacher suicide met with sober reflection
GrossmontCommunity College speech instructor Ed Rintye was almost 90 minutes late for a meeting last Saturday morning at colleague Richard Larson's home.
"So," Larson said, "at about 11:20, I called and got his phone answering machine."
"Call the coroner," he heard Rintye's recorded voice say. "There's been a suicide. I'm sorry that you're the one that's received this message."
Larson promptly called the police, and, a few minutes later, an officer arrived at Rintye's North Park home. He found the 46-year-old teacher slumped in a living room chair, a bullet wound in his head.
Rintye left behind his wife, Patricia, two children from previous marriages, a half-brother in Las Vegas, his mother in Seattle -- and a community of students and colleagues who are still trying to understand one of the most controversial instructors in the junior college's 22-year history.
Just last semester, Patricia Rintye remembers, her husband assigned students in one of his interpersonal relations class to write suicide notes.
"I never found out what the result was of that assignment," she says, but adds that she's sure it had nothing to with her husband's death.
"He took everybody by surprise (with his assignments), and this assignment would have been no more off-the-wall than anything else," she says.
"He liked using theatrical techniques as metaphor, to make a point, to get a message across to the students ... He was always innovating; he was very alert to new information. He loved what he did."
Patricia was Rintye's third wife; they met in the late '70s when she was a student in one of his classes, and were married in 1981. She had not been living with him in recent weeks. "We were separated, but still very much in contact," she says.
She can offer no reasons for what Rintye did last weekend, and she dismisses any suggestion that his classroom manner was symptomatic of any deeper problem.
But she does acknowledge that the idea of suicide was always with her husband. Several of his friends agree.
"We did know that, over the years, suicide was always an alternative in Ed's mind," says Larson, also a speech instructor at Grossmont. "To be close to Ed meant to kind of know that.
"We knew he was upset, but there was no reason to think that this was going to happen ... There was nothing external going on in his life that he couldn't handle. This was an internal choice."
Larson joined Rintye's family for a private memorial service at 3 p.m. yesterday.
Edward Rintye joined the staff at Grossmont on Sept. 10, 1968, after a year of teaching at Cal Western College in Point Loma. He was born in La Porte, Ind., and held a bachelor's degree from Western Michigan University and a master's from San Diego State. He later took a doctorate from U.S. International University. He served with the Marines in Vietnam and reached the rank of captain.
One of his first steps at Grossmont was to embark on "The Idea Program," a course that featured team-teaching in psychology, art, science and speech.
That program lasted about four years, after which he concentrated on his main area of expertise, interpersonal communication. He published a textbook on the subject, "Centering a Lopsided Egg."
"He was into what we called in those days a 'touchy-feely' approach," remembers Ed Claussen, a history teacher at Grossmont and president of the faculty Senate from 1980-82. "About 1972 to 1974, he required students to wear leotards in his class."
The idea was to concentrate on body language, a subject Rintye was very interested in.
"Considering the outrageousness of that requirement, there was only a ripple of discussion about it," Claussen recalls.
Claussen also remembers another technique of Rintye's to get the attention of his students and arouse discussion -- tearing down an American flag and stomping it on the floor.
But neither Claussen, nor other colleagues, ever viewed Rintye's antics as threatening to students.
"To classify Ed as a kook would be inaccurate," says Neil Towne, a friend and colleague in the speech department.
"Ed didn't do anything without a good reason ...
He was a very colorful guy, and he rubbed a lot of people the wrong way. I think he insulted students very often, but he was insulting them to get them to move."
In the process, Towne adds, Rintye taught a lot of students a lot of things.
"There were many who would gladly have become his disciples," he says, "if he had chosen to take disciples."
Towne remembers Rintye appearing in clownface on the first day of a new semester, then launching into a lecture on the many figurative masks people wear every day.
Elaine McLevie, dean of the communication and fine arts department, says Rintye was a good teacher and that she'd never head a major complaint about his style.
"Whenever I talked with him, I came away wanting to read something, to learn something," she says. "He had an ability to make people think about life.
"The problems that he was running into were strictly personal."
Susan Kaveney, a student in Rintye's Speech 120 class last semester, calls him the most eccentric teacher she's ever had.
"I learned a lot from him," she adds. "Everybody was looking forward to coming back and talking to him. He was an excellent teacher -- there was a lot to him."
Kaveney says Rintye donned no particular costumes for her class, nor did he make unusual writing assignments. But he was different.
"He made us at one time go out and four times a day introduce ourselves to a perfect stranger," she recalls. "We had a step-by-step process to do this. We had to get to the boundaries of their personal space."
She also remembers a formal complaint from a group of students concerned about Rintye's frequent profanity in class. After word of it got back to him, Kaveney says, he made a sincere effort to rein in his language. Still, she says, "he talked about sex a lot."
He also cited examples from his personal life as examples in facing theoretical problems. He mentioned to the class that he was separated from his wife and in the process of a divorce, Kaveney says, but didn't dwell on it.
On the Wednesday following Rintye's suicide, Kaveney got together with a classmate to talk about it.
"We were angry with him, because he didn't follow his own advice ... It's so hypocritical. He would talk so much about (having to) learn to deal with your problems. 'Don't cop out,' he said. But no one knows what went through his mind when he did this.
"He's the type of person that if he wanted to do something, he'd just do it -- not think about it, but do it."
Last Friday was full of promises for Rintye.
His Christmas vacation had just begun, and he was planning a trip with Patricia to visit friends up the coast. He and Larson were progressing on a manuscript about interpersonal communication.
That morning, he'd learned that morning of his selection for a computer science scholarship at National University. He cheerfully discussed its possibilities with a student in the mid-afternoon.
About 5 p.m., he talked on the phone with Larson's wife, Sarah, about help he was giving her with a thesis paper.
But, sometime between Friday evening and Saturday morning, Edward Rintye lost interest in the future.
The San Diego Union, 23 December 1983

Army Maj. Ernest "Tex" Ritter 2nd, formerly of Hardwick, died on May 16 from injuries received in an auto accident near Wurzburg, Germany, where he was stationed.
He was 35.
He received his basic training at Fort Sill, Okla., and was assigned to the 1st Battalion of the 15th Field Artillery at Camp Stanley in Korea. He was a fire support and direction officer.
In 1981, he reported to the 1st Battalion of the 29th Field Artillery at Fort Carson, Colo., where he served as a communications platoon leader, battalion motor officer and battery executive officer before completing 26 months as a battery commander.
In 1985, he attended the Infantry Officers Advance Course at Fort Benning, Ga., and was later selected for recruiting duty in Concord, N.H., where he served as a company commander and battalion operations officer.
After attending CAS3 and the Command and General Staff College at Fort Leavenworth, Kansas, he was assigned to the 3rd Infantry Division Artillery in Wurzburg in 1990. He served as material readiness officer.
Last April, he was assigned as executive officer of the 5th Battalion of the 41st Field Artillery.
Born and schooled in Hardwick, he was a 1979 graduate of the University of Massachusetts in Amherst.
He was awarded the Meritorious Service Medal with oak leaf cluster, the Army Commendation Medal, the Army Achievement Medal, the National Defense Service Medal, and the Armed Forces Reserve Medal.
He was a member of First Unitarian Universalist Church and the Sons of American Legion Post 246 in Gilbertville.
He leaves his wife, the former Diane Bayles; three daughters, Heidi, April and Elaine in Germany; his parents, John and Olive Ritter ; his paternal grandmother, Sibyl F. Ritter , all of Hardwick; a sister, Jane R. Comerford of Millis, and a brother, Garrett F. of Centreville, Va.
Military graveside services will be conducted Saturday afternoon in Hardwick Cemetery. Charbonneau Funeral Home is in charge.
Memorial contributions may be made to the Ernest Ritter 2nd Family Scholarship Fund, in care of Diane M. Ritter , Box 124, Hardwick, 01037.
