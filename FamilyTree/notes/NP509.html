Alvin J. SOMMER

[I040529]
Alvin Joseph Sommer, age 87, of Yankton, South Dakota, died Friday, February 12, 2016 at Avera Sister James Care Center in Yankton where he had resided for the past seven years.

A Memorial Mass will be held at 10:30 AM, on Wednesday, February 17, 2016 at Sacred Heart Catholic Church in Yankton with Reverend Larry Regynski officiating. Inurnment will be at the Garden of Memories Cemetery in Yankton with military honors provided by the Ernest-Bowyer VFW Post No. 791 and the SD Army National Guard. The family will receive friends from 5:00 to 8:00 PM, on Tuesday, February 16, at the Wintz & Ray Funeral Home in Yankton with a rosary at 6:45 PM, followed by a vigil service at 7:00 PM.

Alvin was born July 9, 1928, at Ree Heights, SD to Lawrence and Helen (Schaefer) Sommer. He graduated from Miller High School in 1948 and began a 25 year career with the Bureau of Reclamation and Corp of Engineers. During these years, he was employed at the Pactola Dam in Rapid City, SD; helped survey the transmission lines coming from Gavins Point Dam; worked on the San Luis Dam at Los Banos, CA, the Big Bend Dam at Chamberlain, SD and the Chatfield Dam at Englewood, CO. Alvin was also attached to NASA during the construction of the ABM missile site at Cavalier, ND. He retired from the government at the completion of this project. When Alvin retired, he moved his family to Yankton. He worked for two years at the Human Services Center and then for 13 years in the City Engineers Office in Yankton. Alvin served in the United States Army from 1950 to 1953. He fought in several campaigns in Korea including the Iron Triangle. He then went to Hoechst, Germany, where he was assigned to the 7th Army Combat Engineers. While there, his troop was sent to the Netherlands to help with a devastating flood. His troop was awarded a citation from Queen Juliana. Alvin married Laurel Halling on August 8, 1955 at St. Martin's Catholic Church in Huron, SD. They were blessed with four children: Craig, Gary, Allene and Glenn. Alvin was a man of great faith and a very patient and kind person. He was not one to criticize others. His home and family were his pride and joy.
Alvin is survived by his wife of 60 years, Laurel of Yankton; two sons, Craig (Cheryl) Sommer of Yankton and Gary Sommer of Houston, TX; daughter, Allene Sommer (Mark Hausmann) of Yankton; six grandchildren: Jennifer (Derek) Thompson of Ft. Collins, CO, Jack, Brady and Sabrina Sommer of Houston, Alex Heglin of Yankton and Max Eide of Yankton; a great grandson, Kohen Thompson; brother, David (Darlene) Sommer of Lakewood, CO; three sisters, Jenise Marshall of San Manuel, AZ, Adeline Cotton of Yankton and Audrey Campbell of Miller, SD; sister-in-law, Faye Sommer of Sioux Falls; and brothers-in-law, David McNamara of Sioux Falls and James (Debra) Halling of Ft. Paul John SOMMER

[I088492]
Prolific H-B director Paul Sommer dead at 99
Director and animator Paul Sommer, nominated three times for a Daytime Emmy Award for his work on Hanna-Barbera series, died June 29 in Studio City, California of pneumonia. He was 99.
He shared Daytime Emmy nominations in 1988 and 1989 for Outstanding Animated Program for his work as a director of Smurfs. The following year, he shared a third nomination in the same category for directing A Pup Named Scooby-Doo.
From 1933 until his retirement, he worked for TerryToons, MGM, Columbia, Lantz, Swift-Chaplin, TV Spots, Ray Patin, Song Ads, Hanna-Barbera, Pantomime, Bakshi-Krantz, DePatie-Freleng and Filmation. He was sometimes credited as Paul Sommers.
He was a director of H-B's Tom and Jerry Kids Show (1990-92) and an associate producer of the 1973 TV series Butch Cassidy and The Addams Family.
As a director, his many other TV series included Skyhawks (1969); The Flintstone Kids (1986); Snorks, Popeye and Son, Yogi's Treasure Hunt, Pound Puppies, Foofur and The Jetsons (all 1987); The New Yogi Bear Show and The Completely Mental Misadventures of Ed Grimley (both 1988); Fantastic Max (1988-90); Paddington Bear and The Further Adventures of SuperTed (both 1989); The Adventures of Don Coyote and Sancho Panda, Bill & Ted's Excellent Adventures, Potsworth & Co. and Gravedale High (all 1990); The Pirates of Dark Water (1991); and Yo Yogi! (1991-92).
Sommer directed the cartoon TV-movies Yogi's Great Escape, Scooby-Doo Meets the Boo Brothers, Top Cat and the Beverly Hills Cats and Yogi Bear and the Magical Flight of the Spruce Goose (all 1987); Rockin' with Judy Jetson (1988); and Jonny's Golden Quest (1993).
In his younger days, Sommer directed the theatrical shorts King Midas, Junior, The Dumbconscious Mind and Old Blackout Joe (all 1942); The Playful Pest, The Cocky Bantam, The Rocky Road to Ruin, He Can't Make It Stick, The Fly in the Ointment, Professor Small and Mr. Tall and The Vitamin G-Man (all 1943); Mutt 'n' Bones and Lionel Lion (both 1944); and Simple Siren and River Ribber (both 1945).
He animated the shorts The Field Mouse (1941); Wolf Chases Pigs (1942); Catnipped (1944); Phoney Baloney, Ku-Ku Nuts, Fiesta Time and Goofy News Views (all 1945); Picnic Panic and Foxy Flatfoots (both 1946); Tooth or Consequences, Leave Us Chase It and Fowl Brawl (all 1947); Lo, the Poor Buffal, Pickled Puss and Flora (all 1948); and Grape Nutty (1949).
Sommer was an animation director, animator or layout artist for the TV series Quick Draw McGraw and The Huckleberry Hound Show (both 1960), The Yogi Bear Show (1961), The All-New Super Friends Hour (1977), The Flintstone Kids and Pound Puppies (both 1986) and Droopy: Master Detective (1993).
He was an animator and layout artist for the 1971 TV-movie Aesop's Fables. Animation director for the 1989 TV documentary A Yabba-Dabba-Doo Celebration!: 50 Years of Hanna-Barbera, he was a layout artist for the 1961 short Fee Fie Foes and the 1977 TV-movie A Flintstone Christmas.
He animated the 1972 ABC Afterschool Special The Last of the Curlews (for which he was also a story director) and the 1974 movie The Nine Lives of Fritz the Cat.
Sommer was a story director for the H-B series The Huckleberry Hound Show (1960-61); The Yogi Bear Show and Top Cat (both 1961); Quick Draw McGraw (1961-62); The Jetsons (1962); The Flintstones (1963-65); The Magilla Gorilla Show and The Peter Potamus Show (both 1964); Jonny Quest (1964-65); The Secret Squirrel Show and The Atom Ant Show (both 1965); Space Ghost (1966); Birdman, The Herculoids and Abbott & Costello (all 1967); Wacky Races (1968-69); Cattanooga Cats (1969); Harlem Globe Trotters and Scooby Doo, Where Are You! (both 1970); Josie and the Pussycats (1970-71); The Funky Phantom, The Pebbles and Bamm-Bamm Show and Help!... It's the Hair Bear Bunch! (all 1971); The Flintstone Comedy Hour, The Amazing Chan and the Chan Clan, The New Scooby-Doo Movies and Sealab 2020 (all 1972); Wait Till Your Father Gets Home (1972-73); Inch High, Private Eye, Butch Cassidy and Jeannie (all 1973); C B Bears and The All-New Super Friends Hour (both 1977); The All-New Popeye Hour, Yogi's Space Race, Dinky Dog, Scooby's All Star Laff-A-Lympics, Galaxy Goof-Ups and Challenge of the SuperFriends (all 1978); and The New Fred and Barney Show (1979).
In addition, he was a story director for the Loopy De Loop theatrical cartoons Rancid Ransom, Chicken Fracas-See and Bearly Able (all 1962); Chicken Hearted Wolf and Just a Wolf at Heart (both 1963); Bear Knuckles, Trouble Bruin and Elephantastic (all 1964); and Crow's Fete, Pork Chop Phooey and Horse Shoo (all 1965). He story directed the TV-movies A Flintstone Christmas (1977) and The Popeye Valentine Special: Sweethearts at Sea (1979).
Sommer was a storyboard director for The Barkleys and The Houndcats (both 1972), Partridge Family 2200 AD (1974), The New Tom & Jerry Show (1975); The Mumbly Cartoon Show, Clue Club, The Scooby-Doo/Dynomutt Hour and Jabberjaw (all 1976); The Great Grape Ape Show (1977) and Dynomutt Dog Wonder (1988). Sommer was a storyboard editor for Valley of the Dinosaurs and Wheelie and the Chopper Bunch (both 1974), and a storyboard artist for The New Adventures of Batman (1977) and Garfield and Friends (1988).
From 1970 until 1978, he served on the executive board of Local 839 of IATSE, known as The Animation Guild; he chaired a committee that rewrote the union's constitution and bylaws.
He received the Guild's Golden Award in 1984.
Born Paul John Sommer on June 8, 1912, he last lived in North Hollywood.

Phillip Lee SOPER

[I074699]
McHenry Tries To Stop A Parole
McHenry County officials have argued against parole for a man sentenced to two consecutive 150 to 200-year prison sentences for killing two county residents in 1970.
The Illinois Prisoner Review Board has already denied parole seven times for Phillip Lee Soper, 43, who is also serving a concurrent life term for the killing of a Wisconsin nursing home resident.
Soper also has admitted to the murder of a nursing home resident in Ohio but has never been convicted in that case, officials said.
How much more dangerous must a man be before we recognize that prison is the only safe place to keep him from hurting us again, McHenry County First Assistant State's Atty. Michael Vujovich said in a statement.
Soper was convicted in 1971 of the shooting murders of Marlene Ahrens of Woodstock and Guenther Dolenske of McHenry. Soper is serving his sentence at the Menard Correctional Center in Chester, Ill., Vujovich said.
Ahrens was fatally shot Dec. 17, 1970, when Soper surprised her in the back of the dentist's office where she was working as a receptionist. He had intended to rob an adjacent tavern but mistakenly entered the office.
At the same time, Soper shot John Bocker, the owner of the building where the office was located. Bocker survived.
The next day Soper shot Dolenske five times while robbing the Farmhouse Tavern in McHenry. After Soper robbed the bar and its patrons, Dolenske followed him outside to write down the car`s license plate number.
Soper reportedly warned him to stop, but Dolenske refused and he was shot. A short time later Soper was arrested at a McHenry hotel where he had been living for about two months.
Because of scheduling conflicts, Vujovich can not attend Soper's hearing Wednesday, so he appeared before the Prisoner Review Board last week to argue that Soper should never be released.
Soper has been eligible for parole since 1980. He has applied for parole seven times and been denied each time. At his last appearance, in 1988, the board agreed to hear his request every three years.
By Wilson Ring, Chicago Tribune, 20 November 1991
