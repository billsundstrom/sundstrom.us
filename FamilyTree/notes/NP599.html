Burdette Shields WRIGHT

[I072190]
Burdette Shields Wright Jr., 78, a World War II veteran, reporter and CIA employee, died Oct. 2 of a heart attack at his home in Leesburg.
Born in the District, Mr. Wright graduated from the Nichols School in Buffalo, N.Y.
He attended Kenyon College before joining the Army Air Corps in 1942. A bombardier with the 8th Air Force, Mr. Wright was shot down over Europe and taken prisoner by the Germans. He was liberated shortly before V-E Day by advancing Russian forces.
Mr. Wright was awarded the Bronze Star and the Purple Heart for his combat service.
After the war, he earned a bachelor's degree from Georgetown University. After graduation, he was a reporter and assistant editor for the American Aviation Publishing Co. and the Globe Press Syndicate, covering legislation and activities of regulatory agencies. He joined the CIA in the early 1950s, serving in Argentina and Vietnam before retiring in 1973.
From 1974 to 1984, Mr. Wright was the congressional liaison for Los Angeles County in Washington.
He returned to his family's farm in Loudoun County, where he was a member of the Arts Council and a founding member of the Scotch Irish Society in Virginia.
Mr. Wright was a member of Alpha Delta Phi fraternity, the St. Andrew's Society, the Clan Campbell Society, the Army-Navy Club and the Chevy Chase Club.
His first marriage to Helen Ross Lipscomb ended in divorce. He is survived by his wife of 33 years, Suzanne Walker Wright ; their son, Andrew W. Wright of Leesburg; five children from his first marriage, Duncan K. Wright of Mount Airy, Md.; Campbell R. Wright of Royal Oak, Va.; Thomas W. Wright of Ancram, N.Y.; Mary E. Wright of Boulder, Colo.; and Alexander C. Wright of Los Angeles; a sister, Elizabeth W. Goshorn of Leesburg; a stepson, Joseph W. Henderson III of the District; and seven grandchildren. A brother, Philip Campbell Wright , died in 1974.
Funeral services will be held at St. James Episcopal Church in Leesburg at 10 a.m. tomorrow. Interment will be at Arlington National Cemetery at 1 p.m.
Contributions may be made to the Loudoun County Volunteer Rescue Squad, P.O. Box 1277, Leesburg, Va. 20177.
The Washington Times, 19 October 1998

Clarabell WRIGHT

[I061747]
Clarabell W. Finn, 85, of Bisbee, Ariz., entered heaven's gates to join the Lord on Sept. 5, 2014.
She was born in Pampa, Texas, to the late Alvin Ray and Hazel Wright and moved to Bisbee in 1935. She graduated from Bisbee High School in 1947. She attended Compton Community College in Compton, Calif., and graduated in 1949 with a degree in bookkeeping.
She was united in marriage to William (Skeeter) Finn on Apr. 3, 1952, in Bisbee. He preceded her in death on Aug. 28, 2004.
Clarabell was a longtime resident of Cochise Row and was known and loved by many people. She was one of the longest-running members of TOPS in southern Arizona and received an award in 2008. She was a member and volunteer of the Historic and Mining Museum is Bisbee and it was one of her favorite pastimes.
She was an avid member of Grace Lutheran Church and enjoyed attending many church activities and Bible study. She worked at Arizona Bank and owned a neat little second hand store in Old Bisbee called Grandmaʼs Attic. She was full of knowledge about rocks and minerals and loved bird watching. She made quilts with her parents and carried on their legacy with many traditions and talents.
Clarabell touched many lives with her caring and generous heart, and many considered her their third grandmother.
She is survived by her sons, James Finn and his wife Cheryl of Las Vegas, Nev., and Frederick Finn and his wife Kimberly of Bisbee; nine grandchildren, Raechel Finn, Shannon Finn, Ashley Korte, Carley Finn, Leah Bowen, Jennifer Finn, Chelsea Finn, Claire Finn, and Ricki Lee Finn; and eight great-grandchildren, Michael Johnston, Celeste Stowell-Finn, Shyanne Rivera, Russell Finn, Matthew Sietsma, Lyric Whitman, Emily Whitman, and Ashleyʼs unborn baby.
She was preceded in death by her parents, Alvin Ray and Hazel Wright; her sons, Ray Finn and Richard (Rick) Finn; her eldest grandson, Randy M. Finn; her brothers, Alvin Wright and his wife Mildred, and Clell Wright; her nephews, Denny Wright of Chicago, Ill., and Alvin E. Wright of California.
A celebration of life will be held at Grace Lutheran Church, Bisbee, on Monday, Sept. 22, 2014, at 4 p.m., with a potluck and sharing of memories at the church hall following the service.
Graveside burial will be privately held at a later date.
Published in The Sierra Vista Herald, 20 September 2014

Edmund Wright was another of our oldest graduates, belonging to the fourth class. He was born at Easthampton, Mass., on July 1, 1808 (almost four years before Dr. Thompson). His college training was at Williams in the class of 1836, and his theological course at East Windsor followed immediately. From the Seminary he went almost at once into mission work at St. Louis. After three years he was ordained there, and in 1843 was installed over the church at Weston, Mo., in the western part of the state, where he remained six years. The next seven years were spent as a missionary pastor in St. Louis. His longest service was that of District Superintendent for Missouri of the American Bible Society, beginning in 1863 and reaching a full twenty-five years, to 1888. In the time of the war and afterward his labors were particularly fruitful, but that his zeal continued strong to the end is shown by the fact that in his last year in office he traveled almost 25,000 miles and delivered 150 addresses for the cause of Bible dissemination. On his eightieth birthday he resigned, and subsequently removed to Nebraska. In 1893 he went to Seattle, Wash., to live with his daughter, Mrs. William T. Whitney. The testimony borne to the beauty of his character as shown in his last days is that " he was a benediction in our midst." Mr. Wright was married in 1842 to Miss A. F. Hurd, of Bridport, Vt. Their golden wedding was duly celebrated in 1892, and Mrs. Wright survives, with the daughter above named. Mr. Wright.

Edmund Wright, son of Ichabod and Mary Clapp Wright, was born in Easthampton, Mass., July 1, 1808; was graduated from Williams College in 1836, and from the Theological Institute of Connecticut in 1839. Strongly possessed of the missionary spirit he entered at once into mission work at St. Louis, Mo., where after three years of service he was ordained. In 1843 ne was installed pastor at Weston, Mo., and continued in the work there six years. Returning to St. Louis he spent the next seven years as a missionary pastor in that city. During the Civil War his labors were abundant and fruitful. From 1863 to 1888 he was District Superintendent for Missouri of the American Bible Society, which position he filled with conspicuous energy and ability. From his last report, rendered on his eightieth birthday, when he resigned the position, it appears that during the last year of his work he traveled 24,467 miles, visited and revisited 80 Auxiliaries, attended 37 Anniversaries, visited 20 Ecclesiastical bodies, sent 440 official letters, distributed 5,450 official documents, and delivered 139 addresses and sermons. From Missouri he went to Nebraska, and later (1893) to Seattle, Wash., to live with his daughter, Mrs. William T. Whitney. During the latter years of his life he was identified with the Presbyterian Church and was deeply interested in its work. From his home in Seattle, July 20, 1901, a few days after his ninety-third birthday, he was summoned to the larger service. He was married August 11, 1842, to Achsah Fidelia Hurd of Bridport, Vt., who with one daughter survives him. Mr. Hurd occupied an honorable place among that noble company of men of whose work President Roosevelt has so recently spoken these golden words: "Without it the conquest of this continent would have had little but an animal side. Without it the pioneer's fierce and rude virtues and somber faults would have been left unlit by the flames of pure and loving aspiration. Without it the life of this country would have been a life of inconceivably hard and barren materialism. Because of it deep beneath and through the national character there runs the power of firm adherence to a lofty ideal upon which the safety of the nation will ultimately depend. Honor, thrice honor, to those who for three generations, during the period of this people's great expansion, have seen that the force of the living truth expanded as the nation expanded. They bore the burden and heat of the day, they toiled obscurely and died unknown, that we might come into a glorious heritage." One of his family writes of him in these words: " I would like to tell you how much he thought of all his college associations; looking anxiously for the 'Hartford Seminary Record' and reading it as long as he could read at all, these memories were a constant joy to him during his declining years."
Hartford Seminary Record, page 335-336, Volume XII: November, 1901, to August, 1902
