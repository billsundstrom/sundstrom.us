Maybelle Lance HAMMOND

[I075405]
Maybelle Hammond lived her last years in a little house the color of railroad sleeping cars down in the valley of Phalen Creek in St. Paul, Minnesota. Every Saturday she would make her trip up into town to see the Wild West movies. Leaving early in the morning, she would sit all day watching and then return at dark. To do this she had to climb 103 steps up from the valley to get to Main Street. She was then in her nineties.
She was often persuaded to move into a pretty little white house where she could be safer and more accessible in winter. She tried that awhile and then had everything moved back down to her little green house where she could keep a few chickens and hear the trains go by.
She captivated people by stories of the olden days. Her face was fiercely animated; her eyes sometimes quiet as if seeing a vision, then flashing. It was about the ox cart ride to deadwood, South Dakota, the Mendota Minnesota massacre, the sounds of the Dakota prairie at night, and about her first husband, frank sears who had been the first mayor of Webster, South Dakota, and she his wife pictured in silks and satins and ostrich plumed hat.
She had an old cigar store indian statue with painted green and red feathers, somewhat faded and dust laden in one corner of the little green house.
Some said that she had been to too many Wild West shows. During world war 1 she got a job at the northern pacific railroad stripping the upholstery from Pullman cars. Afraid that they would not hire a woman her age she lied that she was 10 years younger. The lie backfired, since at retirement she had to work until she was 75 instead of 65 to collect her pension.
She fell and broke her hip (age in the nineties). She was taken to the hospital, they put a pin in it, she said, and one day when no one was watching she got out of bed, left the hospital, and took the streetcar home.
She used her railroad pass to go to Fargo, North Dakota to visit her daughters. Then take off to see her son and his wife in Virginia somewhere.
She and her first husband Frank Sears probably left Illinois immediately after their wedding with the general exodus to the Deadwood gold rush.
She used to claim that she had seen Abraham Lincoln.
She divorced Frank Sears in 1901 and moved from South Dakota to St. Paul, Minnesota along with her four children. She died at age 93 from cancer.

Montie Leon HAMMOND

MONTIE L. HAMMOND, 82
Montie L. Hammond, age 82, of Grinnell, died on Saturday, Jan. 4, 2014, at St. Francis Manor in Grinnell.
A celebration of his life was held at 10:30 a.m., Saturday, Jan. 11, at the Smith Funeral Home in Grinnell with the Rev. Kathryn Roys, pastor of St. John's Lutheran Church in Grinnell, officiating. Pallbearers were Travis Hammond, Tim Hammond, Brian Kenkel, Dustin Cummings, Pat Supple, Nick Peiffer, Mike Hudnutt and Royal Roland. Interment was in Hazelwood Cemetery in Grinnell.
Memorials may be designated to the St. Francis manor Foundation and mailed in care of the Smith Funeral Home, PO Box 368, Grinnell, Iowa 50112. Memories and condolences may be shared with the family at www.smithfh.com.
Montie Leon Hammond was born on August 26, 1931, in Beaman, to Andy and Zola (Schryver) Hammond. He was raised on the family farm near Beaman and he graduated from Beaman High School in 1949. He was a gifted athlete and was nominated All-State in basketball while playing at Beaman High School.
He was united in marriage to Betty Jean Howard on Oct. 23, 1949, in Grundy Center. They farmed near Conrad until 1959, and then moved to Grinnell, where they owned and operated Wayne's Steak House. A few years later, they purchased Farmers Exchange Company and Grinnell Feed and Grain. He then managed the Malcom Cooperative for nine year before managing the Lime Springs Cooperative in Lime Springs. Upon his retirement from agriculture, Montie was involved in banking and real estate.
Montie was a long-time member of the Grinnell Golf and Country Club and the Grinnell Elks Lodge. He served on the St. Francis Manor Foundation board and was past president of the United Suppliers Company board. He held a passion for flying and acquired his pilot's license at a young age. His enjoyments included playing golf, fishing, pheasant hunting, swimming with his grandchildren in the pool house he and Betty built on the family farm, and going to Palm Desert, Calif., in the winter months.
Montie is survived by his wife of 64 years, Betty J. Hammond of Grinnell; his son, Rick (Valerie) Hammond of Grinnell; two grandsons, Travis(Kristi) Hammond of Haslet, Texas, and Tim (Colleen Schmitt) Hammond of Grinnell; one granddaughter, Tanya (Anthony) Green of Cedar Rapids; five great grandchildren. Whitney (C.J.), Emily, Cristiano, Denzil, and Lily; one great great granddaughter, Audrey; and one brother, Veryl Hammond of Eldora.
He was preceded in death by his parents; and his brother, Delos Hammond.
The Poweshiek County CR, 13 January 2014
