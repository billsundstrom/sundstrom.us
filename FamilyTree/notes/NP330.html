Anita Tillinghast , 88, passed away surrounded by family on Sunday, Dec.13, 2009, in Colorado Springs, Colo. Memorial services will be held at 10 a.m., Thursday, Dec. 17, 2009, in Ford Memorial Chapel, First Baptist Church, Lubbock, with Rev. John Ballard officiating. Graveside services will be held at 2 p.m., in Parklawn Memorial Gardens in Plainview. Family members will receive friends beginning at 9:30 a.m., prior to the memorial service. Please join the family in celebrating Anita 's life.
Anita was born on Aug. 19, 1921 to Willis Raymond and Dora (Coffee) Logan in Dallas. She graduated from Amarillo High School in 1939. She married Ross Wayne Tillinghast in 1939. She had been a member of Central Baptist Church in Lubbock, First Baptist Church in Dallas and was a member of First Southern Baptist Church in Colorado Springs.
Anita was a Lubbock resident for over 35 years. She always cared for others. While in Lubbock, she was a Licensed Vocational Nurse as a private duty nurse, an office nurse for doctors Cross and Morris and for Dr. T.H. Holmes. She was on the Texas Tuberculosis Association Board of Directors for a number of years, she was an organizer for the Mothers March of Dimes and a life member of the Parent and Teachers Association. She was also a member of the "Rocky Mountain Mama's" senior entertainment group while living in Colorado Springs.
Anita was preceded in death by her parents; her husband, Ross of Lubbock; an infant son, Bobby Ray Tillinghast ; and a daughter, Linda Gail Rodgers of Irving.
She is survived by her brother, Willis R. "Sonny" Logan of Lake Dallas, Texas; sons, Jack Tillinghast and wife Theresa of Kanab, Utah and Jimmy Tillinghast and wife Eugenia of Lubbock; and daughters, Judy Guthrie and husband Lee of Colorado Springs and Betty Swintz and husband George of Colorado Springs.
Her grandchildren are Monte Tillinghast , Lonnie Tillinghast , Kim DeBellis, Ryan Rodgers, Raegan Arrington, Amy Olsen, David Price, Daniel Price and Jennifer Bruecker. She has 12 great-grandchildren and numerous nephews, nieces, cousins and friends.

Breauna M. Logan wrote the following a few months before she committed suicide at the age of 13:
Love
Love is the element that everyone has inside of them. Some express it greatly while others keep it bottled up deep inside. No matter if they express it or not it's still there. Sometimes love hurts deeply and other times it fills us with joy. We need love to live, without it we are dead inside.
We all love someone whether a family member, friend, or someone special. So in fact we are all loved, it might not be the person you love, but someone none the less. If you love someone that loves you back then you should cherish it and try not to lose it. Losing love is always hard whether you fall out of love or someone falls out of love with you, but always know you can find someone else to love you back. You can not force someone to love you so don't pretend to be someone that your not because then that person will love who your pretending to be not who you are inside.
If your not in love with someone don't say you are because in the end you'll hurt not only that person but also yourself. But life goes on so you can find your true love, someone that you want to spend the rest of your life with. Love can be cruel but it can also be the best thing in life when your with the one.
Don't abuse love and take it for granted or it will end. So if you love someone tell them so they know how you really feel, and who knows they could love you back.

Laurence John LOGAN

[I098426]
In Loving Memory of Laurence J. Logan , M.D. Mar. 15, 1934 - Apr. 12, 2010 Beloved husband, father, physician and friend, Laurence J. Logan succumbed to pancreatic cancer on April 12. Born to Knute and Marian Logan in Alexandria, MN, he spent his early years in Olympia, WA before entering Stanford University and later Stanford Medical School. Trained in Internal Medicine, he served as Lt. Colonel-US Army, Chief-Dept. of Medicine in Heidelberg, Germany and Chief-Dept. of Clinical Research at Letterman Hospital in San Francisco. After training in Hematology at USC School of Medicine, he joined the Gundersen Clinic in La Crosse, WI. There he enjoyed patient care and teaching and was recognized as President of the Medical Staff and Teacher of the Year. In 1987, he joined the Hemophilia Center at Huntington Hospital and Orthopaedic Hospital in Los Angeles, where he served as Director. His enthusi asm for caring extended into the community. He spent numerous years as a physician at a summer camp for youth with hemophilia, as President of the Southern California Hemophilia Foundation and President of the South Pasadena Rotary Club. A jazz aficionado, avid reader, historian and international traveler, his legacy of compassion, intellectual honesty and appreciation of life will live on as inspiration to devoted family and friends. Loving family who shared his life are Pauline Logan , wife of 54 years, daughters Vicki, Becky, P.K., and Lauretta as well as eight grandchildren. Should friends desire, donations in memory of Laurence Logan may be made to the Southern California Hemophilia Foundation's summer camp. A celebration of his life will be held at The Neighborhood Church in Pasadena on May 30th.
Pasadena Star-News, 18 April 2010

OʼBrien Straatmann Redinger Funeral Home is in charge of arrangements.
Anna Mae was born to Fred Walker Long and Inez Pearl (Williamson) Long on March 26, 1916. Her father worked for the Union Pacific Railroad for over 40 years. Anna Mae was the oldest of four children. She has a sister, Fern Bieck of Lincoln, Nebraska .
Anna Mae graduated from Lexington High School in Lexington, Nebraska in 1934. After graduation she attended cosmetology school in Omaha, Nebraska and received her license.
She married Alvin Clemans on January 17, 1937. Alvin also worked for the Union Pacific Railroad. They had two children, a son Charles and daughter Judy. She owned her own beauty salon for several years. The marriage ended in divorce on February 19, 1951.
While working in a bakery in Kearney, Nebraska she met and married Alfred Caldwell on August 8, 1952 and moved to Hastings, Nebraska.
In March of 1957 the family moved to Arvada, Colorado. Mr. Caldwell passed away on July 19, 1975.
Anna Mae worked for A.E. Heinsohn as a bookkeeper for 20 years. She retired in March of 1981.
After she retired she bought dolls and would make each doll clothes. At Christmas time she would donate the dolls and clothes to children in need. She did this for many years until her eyesight started to fail.
She is survived by her son, Charles (Lynda) Clemans of Littleton, CO; daughter Judy (Ron) Sorensen of Lakewood, CO; grandchildren, Nannette Vandenack, Kerri Morrison and John Hafkey; great grandchildren, Drew, Allison and Brett Vandenack, Alex Hafkey, and Hailee, Meghan, Katie and Jack Morrison; a sister Fern Biek; a cousin Dora Day; and several cousins and friends.

Dixie loved being a waitress and did so for 40 years. The last 24 years she worked at Rusty's Pancake House in Ontario. She made many friends there and had customers who would not go there if she was not working at the time. You weren't a stranger around Dixie very long.
Dixie loved her family and especially her grandchildren. Most weekends one or two of them would be at her house. She also had a love for dogs. She would cart home strays, the uglier the better. She would get up at 4 a.m. on Saturday to watch a dog show. She also loved to go to Jackpot, Nev. Her and her two sisters tried to go at least once a year.
