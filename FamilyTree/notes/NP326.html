Edith Anna Margaret LINDBERG

[I055284]
Mrs. Edith Anna Margaret Walberg (nee Lindberg), age 90 years, a resident of Grandview Lodge, Thunder Bay, went to be with her Lord on Wednesday, October 2, 2002. She was born September 7, 1912, and lived most of her life in Slate River. Before moving to Grandview Lodge, she resided at 125 S. Edward Street and Jasper Park Lodge.
Edith's main interests were her family, whom she dearly loved, and her church, Slate River Baptist, where she faithfully served for many years. In 1990 Edith received an award from the city of Thunder Bay for her service as organist for over 50 years. She and Renus enjoyed travelling and spending time at their camp at Cloud Bay.
Edith was predeceased by her beloved husband, Renus, on July 21, 1984. She was also predeceased by her mother, Olga Schutte, her father Charles Lindberg, and her stepfather Ernest Schutte; brothers Folke, Jack and Connie.
Edith is survived by her son Ronald (Margot) of Thunder Bay, granddaughter Wendy (Donald Lobb) of Etobicoke,Ontario, great grandchildren Heather, Emily and Nora; granddaughter Leslie (Patrick Hegan) of Orono, Ontario, great grandchildren Connor, Gibson and Alyson; grandson David of Toronto, Ontario; grandson Murray (Loretta) of Burlington, Ontario, great grandchildren Amanda and Megan; son Weldon (Mary) of Port Hope, Ontario, granddaughter Anne (Shane Matthews), great grandchildren Robert, Nicholas and Daniel; granddaughter Cheryl (Brian Boyd) of Auckland, New Zealand, grandsons Matthew and Michael of Port Hope, Ontario; daughter Diane (David Harri) of Thunder Bay, grandchildren Daniel Fuglesang of Malmo, Sweden, Jonathan Fugelsang of White River Junction, Vermont and Mark Fugelsang (Amber) of Thunder Bay, Ontario, and great granddaughter Natasha. She is also survived by her sister-in-law Rose Walberg, and many nieces and nephews.
A Service of Remembrance for the late Mrs. Edith Walberg will be held on Monday, October 7, 2002 at 3:00 P.M. in the Slate River Baptist Church, 80 McCluskey Drive with Hugh Wilson officiating. A private family interment will be held at Mountain View Cemetery. Visitation for family and friends will be held on Sunday, October 6, 2002 from 3:00 until 5:00 P.M. in the WESTFORT CHAPEL, 420 W. Gore Street at James.

Bernard Lindstrom, 91, long-time resident of Grays Lake, passed away peacefully Monday, January 21, 2013 at The Gardens of Rigby.
He was born July 27, 1921 in Soda Springs at the Enders Hotel. His parents were Albin and Lillie Agnes Burton Lindstrom. Albin came from Sweden and was mining on Caribou Mountain.
He brought his washing down to Eagle Creek where he met Lillie Burton, who was recently widowed with 4 children, Lyle, Lucille, Mona and Lloyd. Albin and Lillie were married and had two sons, Albin Jr. and Bernard.
Bernard served in the U.S. Army during WWII. He served in France and Germany and was in Germany when the war ended.
On May 7, 1947 he married Betty Thomas and they had three children, Varden (Butch), Raelene, and Dorene. Bernard and Betty ranched in Grays Lake for 70 years and then moved to Iona in 2008.
He is survived by his children: son, Varden (Butch) (Frances) Lindstrom of Hastings, Michigan; daughters, Raelene (Mick) Humpherys of Layton, Utah, and Dorene (Joe) Shaffer of Iona, Idaho; sister, Mona Worth of Pocatello, Idaho; seven grandchildren; 13 great-grandchildren; six stepgrandchildren; and eight stepgreat-grandchildren.

Carl Lindstrom put compassion to use
He spent his early boyhood on a farm in the Skagit Valley. As a young man, he worked as a seaman, roaming the world from Sweden to China. Then, preparing to settle down with a Seattle bride, he entered a seminary and emerged a Baptist minister.
Life was quite a journey for the Rev. Carl E. Lindstrom, who died July 28 at the age of 94 at the Covenant Shores Retirement Center on Mercer Island.
Throughout his postings as an Army chaplain and a minister in small Washington towns, family members say the Rev. Lindstrom displayed a deep sense of compassion. He valued service over money and material possessions. Never amassing large personal wealth, he was always ready to give to others.
"I'm sure that hobos had our door marked," the Rev. Lindstrom's son David said. "They would always find their way to the house and ask for lunch, and he usually gave them food or money. Once, they were even given my father's overcoat."

The Rev. Lindstrom had his own share of hard times. He was born in Seattle on March 27, 1909, the only child of Swedish immigrant parents. The family moved to a Skagit Valley homestead near Burlington, where they struggled to make a living off the land while living in a one-room house lit by a kerosene lantern.
His mother died as a result of the 1918 flu epidemic, and young Carl was sent to live with his aunt and uncle, Hokan and Victoria Olson, in Seattle. There, he was like a big brother to the Olsons' daughter, Margaret.
"He was a very good student," recalls Margaret Olson Pryne. "So when I got to high school and got a B, I always heard about it. How Carl always got A's."
After graduating from Ballard High School in 1926, he decided to sample life as a merchant seaman. He crewed ships that took him up and down the West Coast, to China where he visited the Great Wall and to Sweden in 1928. There, in the homeland of his parents, he jumped ship to journey north to visit relatives. He stayed on for six months before finding work aboard another ship that took him back to the United States.
Back in the States, he could not forget a Swedish girl - Eleanor Gustafson - whom he'd met in Seattle. He wanted to marry her. But being a merchant seaman, at that time, was not a career considered respectable enough, according to David Lindstrom.
The Rev. Lindstrom, raised in the Swedish Baptist Church, acquired deep religious convictions. So he decided to become a pastor. He enrolled at the Eastern Baptist Theological Seminary in Pennsylvania.
All the while, he continued to court Gustafson. Once he hitchhiked and rode the rails all the way to Seattle for a visit, according to his son. In 1935, the Rev. Lindstrom finally married Gustafson; they had three children and shared 65 years of marriage.
In their early years together, he worked as a pastor at several small churches in New Jersey. Then he signed up for duty as an Army-Air Force chaplain during World War II, continuing to serve through the Korean War.
After leaving the service, the Rev. Lindstrom served as a pastor in many small Washington communities, including Kettle Falls, Oroville, Marcus, Burlington and Kittitas. He felt strongly that different denominations should get along, and he worked to bring different churches together in the small towns where he lived.
He never gave up on education. In Kittitas, he enrolled at Central Washington State University and - at the age of 62 - received a master's degree in English literature.
In 1985, the Rev. Lindstrom retired and settled with his wife in Kirkland. They moved to Covenant Shores in 1995; Eleanor Lindstrom died in 2001.
The Rev. Lindstrom is survived by his daughter, Alice Lindstrom Davis of San Francisco; his son Carl David Lindstrom and daughter-in-law Sandra of Mercer Island; his son Dr. James Lindstrom and daughter-in-law Diane of Yakima; as well as six grandchildren. He also is survived by his cousin, Margaret Olson Pryne of Redmond. A graveside service will be at 10 a.m. Aug. 14 at Lake View Cemetery on Capitol Hill, with a memorial service at 2 p.m. at Covenant Shores Retirement Center. Memorial gifts can be made to a charity of choice.
By Hal Bernton, Seattle Times, 6 August 2003

Bernice Lindstrom Loshbaug, 81, died at Providence Everett Hospital on February 20, 2009, after a long illness. She was born on December 11, 1927, in McPherson, Kansas, to Charles and Minerva Lindstrom.
She attended the McPherson schools and graduated from McPherson College in 1949 with a BA degree in Biology. She married her college sweetheart, Royce Loshbaugh , in 1948.
Bernice is survived by her devoted husband of 60 years; two daughters, Sandy Smith and Pat Bostwick; and sons-in-law, Tim and Don, respectively.
She joined PEO in 1963 and used that organization to connect her life as she moved from homes in Connecticut, New Jersey, Texas and Washington.
Bernice taught high school English before starting their family. Frequent moves, 27 from her own records, prevented her from continuing a teaching career. Instead, she took a number of college accounting courses and handled the family finances and tax returns. She worked with the AARP Tax Aide Program until her massive stroke in 1996. Bernice was also a 30 year survivor of ovarian cancer.
A celebration of Bernice's life will be held at 1:30 p.m., Friday, March 6, at the Snohomish United Methodist Church.
The family has requested in keeping with Bernice 's desire, that in lieu of flowers, donations be made to the following: Cancer Research Institute, 55 Broadway, Suite 1802, New York, NY 10006.
