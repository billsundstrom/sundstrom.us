Charles Elliot HYATT

[I084422]
General Charles L. Hyatt, for nearly half a century president and commandant of the Pennsylvania Military College at Chester, died yesterday at his home on the campus of heart disease. He was seventy-eight.
The general had been ill for several weeks. When he passed away his four children were at his beside. His wife died six years ago. General Hyatt died at 2. A.M. and shortly after revelry yesterday morning the flag on the college campus was lowered to half mast by a color squad composed of Cadet Corporal William Challenger, Jr. of Nanticoke, Pa.; Cadet Stephen Tracy of Oak Lane, and Cadet Richard K Kettlewell of Detroit.
Funeral services will be held tomorrow afternoon at 2 o'clock at P. M. C. The officiating clergymen will be Bishop Francis M. Taitt of the Episcopal Church and a member of the P. M. C. board of directors, and Rev. Dr. A. L. Latham, pastor of the Third Presbyterian Church. Chester. The entire cadet corps of the academy will accompany the body to the interment In Chester Rural Cemetery.
Was Son of Educator
General Hyatt was born in Wilmington, Delaware, in 1851, the son of Colonel Theodore Hyatt, a noted educator, who was head of the Delaware Military Academy, which sent officers to both the Union and Confederate armies at the outbreak of the Civil War.
It was soon after the opening of the war when the academy was removed to West Chester, Pa. General Hyatt, then a small boy, was broken-hearted when his father refused to let him join volunteers to repel the invasion of General Robert E. Lee into Pennsylvania.
In 1867 the academy was removed to Chester, and shortly after this the name was changed to the Pennsylvania Military College, with collegiate powers and privileges invested by the Court of Common Pleas of Delaware County.
In 1872 General Hyatt was graduated with the degree of civil engineer. The only surviving member of his class it Truxton Beale, distinguished American diplomat, who served as minister plenipotentiary to Greece, Persia, Rumania and Serbia. Following his graduation. General Hyatt became an instructor in mathematics, elocution and tactics. In 1873 he was appointed adjutant and in 1876 treasurer and professor of rhetoric. In 1881 be became vice president and in 1887 he succeeded to the presidency of the institution upon the death of his father.
Honored by Pinchot
Seven years ago Gilford Pinchot, then Governor, in recognizing General Hyatt a services in the training of young men for peace and war brevetted him brigadier-general.
General Hyatt was the oldest active college head in the United States. He had conferred honorary degrees upon many public men, including the late President Harding, Chief Justice Charles K. Hughes, the late General Leonard Wood, John J. Pershing, Admiral Richard E. Byrd, Dwight F. Davie, Governor Franklin D. Roosevelt, of New York, and Governor Fisher.
General Hyatt married Miss Kesia West Dyer of Cheater, in 1880. She died in 1924. The general is survived by his four children. Miss Mabel Lee Hyatt. Mrs. Milo C. Burt, of Stratford, Conn, Colonel Frank K. Hyatt, vice president and treasurer of P. M. C., and Leslie S. Hyatt, of Chester.
The Philadelphia Inquirer, 10 April 1939

Owain ap HYWEL

[I004625]
From Wikipedia, 12 June 2006:

Owain ap Hywel (died 987) was king of Deheubarth in south Wales and probably also controlled Powys.

Owain was the son of Hywel Dda, originally king of Deheubarth but by the end of his life king of most of Wales. On Hywel's death in 950 Deheubarth was shared between Owain and his two brothers, Rhodri and Edwin. The sons of Hywel were not able to keep hold of Gwynedd, which was reclaimed for the traditional dynasty of Aberffraw by Iago ab Idwal and Ieuaf ab Idwal, the sons of Idwal Foel.

In 952 Iago and Ieuaf invaded the south, penetrating as far as Dyfed. The sons of Hywel retaliated by invading the north in 954, reaching as far north as the Conwy valley before being defeated in a battle at Llanrwst and being obliged to retreat to Ceredigion.

Rhodri died in 953 and Edwin in 954, leaving Owain to rule Deheubarth alone. Owain did not again try to reclaim Gwynedd, but instead he and his son Einon turned eastwards to attack the kingdom of Morgannwg (modern Glamorgan) in 960, 970 and 977. Owain was now ageing, and it appears that Einon took over the rule of the kingdom on behalf of his father. On a further raid on the east in 984, Einon was killed by the noblemen of Gwent.

Following Einon's death, Owain's second son, Maredudd, took over the leadership in war, and in 986 did what his father had failed to do by seizing the throne of Gwynedd, ousting Ieuaf's son Cadwallon ab Ieuaf. The following year Owain died and Maredudd became king of Deheubarth as well as Gwynedd.

Cadfan ap IAGO

[I008709]
From Wikipedia

Cadfan ap Iago (c. 580-625; reigned from c. 615) (Latin: Catamanus; English: Gideon) was a King of Gwynedd. The son of King Iago, he likely assumed the crown of Gwynedd around 615, shortly after the Battle of Caer-Legion (also known as Chester), during which the forces of Gwynedd were defeated by Æthelfrith of Bernicia. Despite Æthelfrith's military victories, Cadfan continued to provide haven for Edwin of Northumbria, whom Æthelfrith regarded as a major threat.

Cadfan was generally considered to have been a wise and just ruler, noted for his ability to maintain the rule of law and peace during an increasingly hostile period in British history. His memorial stone at Llangadwaladr Church in Anglesey refers to him as "the wisest and most splendid of all kings." He was succeeded by his son Cadwallon.

He was one of the last of the legendary kings of the Britons as accounted by Geoffrey of Monmouth.
