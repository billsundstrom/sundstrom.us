William Titus SICHI

[I074827]
Colonel William T. Sichi, Retired, US Army Medical Corps, was survived by his wife of 43 years, Jean Truly Sichi, his son Andrew W. Sichi from a previous marriage to Hazel Wyatt Sichi, daughter, daughter-in-law Dianne Craig Sichi, and numerous nieces and nephews.
Doctor Sichi was born in New York City on August 18, 1906, only son born to Andrea and Adella Sichi. His parents emigrated from Florence, Italy in 1900. Andrea Sichi was a master sculptor who led the creative stonecutting for the National Cathedral in Washington.
Doctor Sichi completed his undergraduate studies at the University of Maryland before entering the George Washington University School of Medicine. He received his Doctor of Medicine degree in 1931. Internship was completed at nearby Garfield Hospital. He entered the United States Army in 1934 and served as General Medical Officer until his selection for residency training in ophthalmology at Walter Reed General Hospital.
At the time World War II began Dr. Sichi was stationed at Gorgas Army Hospital in the Panama Canal Zone. Dr. Sichi received orders to assume command of a combat hospital in New Guinea. Following the war Dr. Sichi received additional ophthalmology training at University of Pennsylvania and became a Diplomate of the American Board of Ophthalmology in 1947. It is noted that Doctor Sichi was the first active duty military eye surgeon to become Board certified. As chief of Ophthalmology at Valley Forge General Hospital, Doctor Sichi was responsible for the post-war care and visual rehabilitation of thousands of injured war veterans.
The Korean conflict erupted while Doctor Sichi was traveling to his next assignment, Brooke General Hospital at Fort Sam Houston. Brooke became a major casualty referral center, and Doctor Sichi was responsible for mobilizing a full clinical and academic teaching department to manage the enormous volume of patients. Doctor Sichi established the region's first ophthalmology residency training program at Brooke General Hospital. Many of San Antonio's finest eye surgeon's received their training at Brooke, many under the watchful instruction of Doctor Bill Sichi.
Colonel Sichi completed his 21-year military career in 1955 while stationed in Europe as Chief of the Eye Service at both the 130th General Hospital Heidelberg as well as the Army General Hospital in Frankfurt. He was Chief Ophthalmology Consultant for the European Theatre.
Doctor Sichi returned to the Alamo City to open a vigorous civilian practice. He remained close to military medicine, and served as civilian consultant to Brooke Army Medical Center until 1977 whereupon the US Army Surgeon General designated him Consultant Emeritus.
Doctor Sichi loved all sports, but his favorite pastime was golf. He played regularly at Fort Sam Houston and area golf courses, and in later years found enormous pleasure watching all of the televised competitions.
Professional memberships and associations include the American Medical Association, the Society of Military Ophthalmologists, the San Antonio Society of Ophthalmology, the American Academy of Ophthalmology, and the Bexar County Medical Society. He was greatly admired for his compassionate approach to patients, his encyclopedic knowledge, and his keen sense of humor.
Doctor Bill Sichi was a voracious reader: history, biographies, military campaigns, and fiction. He remained in touch with the past trainees and colleagues. Every year the new residents were introduced to Doctor Sichi in order to better grasp the living history of the Brooke program, as well as the strength and intelligence of its founder. We shall miss him greatly.

Stu was born Nov. 6, 1936 in Brooten Minn. to Elmer Frank Sievert and Esther Anna (Wenlund) Sievert. He attended Grove Lake grade school, and graduated from Glenwood High School in 1954. Following graduation, he was employed briefly as a lineman for a telephone company. In 1956, he began working for Northern Pacific Railroad, which was the start of a "very interesting" 36-year career with the Northern Pacific, Burlington Northern, and Burlington Northern-Santa Fe Railroads. Working in a variety of capacities, he retired in 1992 as the General Forman of the Mechanical Dept. in Superior, Wis. at the BNSF Railroad. His proudest achievement, begun in 1983 and completed in 1985, was helping to design and build the first complete, automatic-fueling and lubricating diesel locomotive engine for the Burlington Northern Railroad. In Stuart始s own words, his career was one that he "would not trade for anything!" Stu married Lorraine Ethel "Becky" McCarthy on December 9, 1988, after a lengthy and committed long-distance romance. He enjoyed collecting railroad artifacts and memorabilia, and relaxing, tinkering, and hunting at their cabin in Wascott, Wis. A talented woodworker, Stu took pride in crafting toys, walking sticks and other fun and useful projects for family and friends. His favorite activities included playing golf with his buddies, and spending family time with his beloved bride, Becky and their children, grandchildren and great-grandchildren. Stuart is preceded in death by his father, Elmer; his mother, Esther; and his brother, Stanley. Bobby Charles SIGLER

[I076187]
TORRINGTON - A celebration of the life of Torrington resident Bob C. Sigler, 79, will be held from noon to 2 p.m. Monday, Sept. 12, in the Holiday Inn Express reception room in Torrington.
Cremation has taken place under the direction of Colyer Funeral Home of Torrington and private family burial was conducted.
He died Sept. 9, 2005, at Torrington Community Hospital.
Born Oct. 25, 1925, in Naper, Neb., he was the son of Eva (Hammond) and Clifford Sigler; was raised and educated in various small towns in Nebraska, South Dakota and Wyoming; and graduated from Upton High School in 1943. He enjoyed playing basketball.
He enlisted in the U.S. Navy and was assigned to the U.S.S. Berrien (APA-62) in 1944, serving until the end of World War II.
After his honorable discharge, he attended the University of Wyoming and received a bachelor's degree from the College of Business in 1949 and his juris doctorate from the College of Law in 1952. For a short time afterward, he worked for the Department of Highways in Cheyenne.
He then established a private law practice in Torrington and continued for more than 50 years to serve the legal needs of residents in Wyoming, Nebraska, and surrounding states. At one time, he was a U.S. magistrate. He also practiced with his good friend, former Gov. Stan Hathaway, for several years.
His interests included pheasant-hunting; walking with his dogs on his land; trips to the Black Hills of South Dakota with his family; and, especially, going to garage sales. He had been a member of the Wyoming Bar Association, Free Masons, Veterans of Foreign Wars, and First Wyoming Presbyterian Church of Torrington.
Survivors include his long-time companion and friend, Kathy Moriarty of Torrington; three children and their spouses, Marie Sorensen of Grants Pass, Ore., Brent Sigler of Lone Tree, Colo., and Kay McKim of Anchorage, Alaska; sister, Neva Jane "Janie" Hamm, and her husband of Tyler, Texas; eight grandchildren; 10 great-grandchildren; and numerous nieces and nephews.
He was preceded in death by his parents; a son, David Bruce; a daughter, Janice Clare; a brother, Derald Eugene; and a sister, Norma Jean.
The family would appreciate memorials to the Gertrude Burns Scholarship Fund, in honor of his beloved teacher; or the Bob C. Sigler Scholarship Fund for UW College of Law. Either may be sent in care of Colyer Funeral Home, P.O. Box 20, Torrington, WY 82240.
Star Tribune, Casper, 11 September 2005
