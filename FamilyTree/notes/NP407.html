Kathleen H. (Orr) Slinden, 88, of Modesto, California, died June 23, 2012 at Alexander Cohen Hospice House in Hughson, California surrounded by her four daughters, her five grandchildren, her eight great-grandchildren, and her great-great-grandson.
Mrs. Slinden was born February 28, 1924 in Dakota County, Minnesota, the daughter of Lee and Norma Orr. She was the third of five daughters and grew up in Minnesota, graduated from Teachers' Training and taught school there before moving to California. In 1944 while working at Loomis Flower Shop in Modesto, she met Pelvin Slinden and on January 23, 1945 they were married in Modesto followed by a honeymoon through the Gold Country of Sonora and Columbia. They moved to Pelvin's hometown of Atwater, Minnesota where they worked together raising their four daughters on a small family farm. In 1960 they moved to California to be near her family in Stanislaus/Merced County and they lived in Central California until 1966 when they moved to Bellevue, Washington. In 1971 they returned to Turlock/Modesto where they were active in the Calvary Lutheran Church in Modesto. They later retired to Angels Camp where Pelvin passed away in November, 1991. In 2005, Mrs. Slinden moved to Casa de Modesto in Modesto, California where she made many friends and joined the Red Hat Society. In the Spring of 2012 she became ill and eventually was transferred to the Alexander Cohen Hospice House in Hughson where she peacefully passed away as the sun was rising Saturday morning.
Mrs. Slinden is survived by three of her sisters, Dorothy Blumenstein of Turlock, CA, Mildred Badenschier of Austin, MN, and Donna Kinney of Santa Cruz, CA; her four daughters and their spouses, Karen Carlson of Sierra Vista, AZ, Lynae Slinden of Whidbey Island, WA, Marlys and Richard Powers of San Ramon, and Patricia and Michael Denney of Twain Harte, and their families.

Julia Hypatia ORTH

[I035349]
Julia Hypatia Orth, 24, was killed instantly in a car accident on I-70 near Boonville, MO, on July 11, 2006.
She was a brilliant young woman of incredible talent and enthusiasm. Homeschooled as a teenager in Cedar Hill, MO, she considered learning a lifelong pursuit, and never stopped trying or discovering new things. She was an avid reader, writer, drawer, painter, knitter, swimmer, runner, juggler, musician, and computer programmer, just to name a few. She loved animals, starting with the pets she kept as a child (including chickens, cats, and a border collie she trained herself), and continuing with the dolphins she studied at New College of Florida, where she received a bachelor's degree in biopsychology. It was her intention to become a research scientist in animal cognition, behavior, and communication, to which end she was about to begin graduate studies leading to a PhD at the University of Tennessee. She touched many lives both personally and through her work, and leaves behind a network of friends and admirers all around the world. She was passionate about environmental issues, and worked tirelessly to cultivate greener habits in herself and all who knew her. For anyone who could spare just a few dollars or a couple minutes of their time, she made the following suggestions, any one of which would ease the burden on what she saw as a beautiful planet in need of care:
*Replace an incandescent light bulb with a fluorescent, or even better, an LED bulb.
*Walk some place you normally drive.
*Buy carbon offset credits for long car or plane trips (http://www.carbonfund.org/).
*Plant a tree.
*Ask your mayor to join the U.S. Mayor's Climate Protection Agreement, or thank them if they already have (http://www.ci.seattle.wa.us/mayor/climate/default.htm#how).
*Tell your representatives you support Rep. Henry Waxman's Safe Climate Act (http://www.henrywaxman.house.gov/waxman/safeclimate/index.htm).
*Talk to your friends.
Julia's was not a message of doom, but one of gentle and hopeful insistence that even the largest problems are surmountable if everyone contributes just a little. She did her part, and had faith that others would begin to follow the example she set.
She was a former member of the Weavers' Guild of St. Louis and continued her weaving, spinning, knitting and natural dyeing while she lived in Sarasota, Florida and Portland, Oregon. She was also a member of the St. Louis Homeschool Network and was previously featured in the Meramec Journal and the St. Louis Post-Dispatch when she became a National Merit Scholar in 1999.Services: At a future date there will be a celebration of her life in Port Townsend, Washington and Sarasota, Florida.
Julia was survived by her beloved fiance, Nicholas Blanchard-Wright, Port Townsend, WA; her parents, Robert and Anne Orth and brother, Michael R. Orth, all of Gerald, MO; other relatives and many friends.
The family was served by Oltmann Funeral Home, Washington, MO.
St. Louis Post-Dispatch 26 July 2006
